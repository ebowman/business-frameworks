# Financial Fluency Reference

A synthesized guide to reading, interpreting, and acting on the three core financial statements, drawn from Section School's Financial Fluency courses. Designed as a reference for anyone who needs to participate confidently in finance-related conversations, build business cases, or make strategic decisions.

---

## Why Financial Fluency Matters

Financial statements are not math -- they are interpretation, analysis, and deduction. You are not looking at facts; you are looking at someone's structured opinions backed by numbers. There are few universally "right" answers. Different readers with different backgrounds will see different stories in the same table.

### What financial fluency enables you to do

- Hold meaningful conversations about revenue and cost with CFOs and finance teams
- Build compelling business cases that connect your work to the bottom line
- Contribute to strategic planning: cost-cutting, fundraising, budgeting, acquisitions
- Influence organizational decisions by positioning yourself as a voice of reason grounded in data

### Who needs it

Everyone -- from individual contributors to C-suite executives, from startups to large corporations, from HR to strategy to operations. If your superiors' performance indicators are tied to the bottom line, you need to understand what that bottom line is.

---

## The SpeeD-Up Framework: How to Read Any Financial Statement

A two-step methodology for quickly analyzing any financial statement:

**Step 1: Spot Key Terms (Issue-Spot)**
- What am I looking at? Which statement is this?
- What terms can I recognize?
- Find the biggest numbers and anchor yourself there

**Step 2: Draw Conclusions (Vertical and Horizontal Analysis)**
- Go "up and down" within a single period (vertical analysis) to understand proportions
- Go "left and right" across time periods (horizontal analysis) to understand trends
- Compare against industry peers (competitive benchmarking / "comps")

## Statement 1: The Income Statement (P&L)

### What it is

The income statement -- also called the P&L (Profit and Loss), earnings statement, statement of operations, statement of financial results, or statement of financial income -- shows your revenues and expenses over a discrete period of time.

**Core question it answers:** "Is my business working? Is it profitable?"

### The eight key terms, in order

| Term | Definition | Example (Ice Cream Shop) |
|------|-----------|--------------------------|
| **Revenue (Top Line)** | Total sales income from the business | Sales of cones, sundaes, milkshakes |
| **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)** | Direct costs to produce what you sell. COGS are NOT classified as "expenses" on a P&L. | Ice cream tubs, cones, toppings, cups |
| **Gross Profit (Gross Margin)** | Revenue minus COGS. Also called profit margin. | What remains after paying for ingredients |
| **SG&A (Selling, General & Administrative Expenses)** | Operating expenses: payroll, rent, marketing, utilities | Salaries for scoopers, shop rent, advertising |
| **EBITDA** | Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. Everything before those deductions. | Operating profit before non-cash charges and financing |
| **Depreciation** | Reduction in value of tangible assets over time (deterioration) | Wear on freezers, furniture, shop equipment |
| **Amortization** | Spreading the cost of long-term intangible or fixed assets over their useful life | Cost of a patent or long-term lease |
| **Net Income (Bottom Line)** | What remains after all expenses, interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization | The actual profit (or loss) of the business |

### Key relationships

```
Revenue
 - COGS
= Gross Profit
 - SG&A
= EBITDA
 - Depreciation & Amortization
= Operating Profit (EBIT)
 - Interest & Tax
= Net Income (Bottom Line)
```

**Important note:** Managers are generally responsible for everything above the EBITDA line. Below that line takes sleuthing with the finance team.

### How to analyze an income statement

**Vertical Analysis (within a single period):**
- What percentage of revenue goes to COGS? To SG&A? To net income?
- Are expenses proportionally reasonable?

**Horizontal Analysis (across periods):**
- Is revenue growing or shrinking quarter-over-quarter or year-over-year?
- Are expenses growing faster than revenue?

**Competitive Benchmarking:**
- Look up competitors' P&Ls (these are public for listed companies)
- Compare margins and expense ratios
- Identify ways to make your business more efficient
- Especially valuable for startups, since investors want to know what your "comps" look like

### Limitations

A P&L cannot tell you if your company is healthy in the broader sense. It will not warn you if the company is about to go bankrupt. For that, you need the balance sheet.

## Statement 2: The Balance Sheet

### What it is

The balance sheet -- also called the statement of financial position -- is a snapshot of the financial health of a company at a specific point in time. It shows what you own, what you owe, and your book value.

**Core question it answers:** "Am I bankrupt? Should I invest?"

### The fundamental equation

```
Assets = Liabilities + Shareholder Equity
```

This is why it is called a "balance" sheet -- both sides must always equal each other. It can be presented in long form (top to bottom) or T-shape (assets on the left, liabilities plus equity on the right).

### Key components

**Assets (what you own):**

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| Current Assets | Cash, accounts receivable (invoices due within 12 months), inventory |
| Non-Current Assets | Property, plants, equipment, long-term investments |

**Liabilities (what you owe):**

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| Current Liabilities | Accounts payable (amounts owed to vendors due soon) |
| Non-Current Liabilities | Long-term bank loans, bonds payable |

**Shareholder Equity:**
The residual value -- what would be returned to shareholders if all assets were liquidated and all debts were paid off. Although we associate equity with ownership, it falls on the liabilities side of the balance sheet equation.

### Why balance sheets matter

- Assess both near-term and long-term financial health
- Show how much debt a company has taken on and how leveraged it is
- Enable competitive benchmarking against industry peers
- Crucial for investors and top management executives

### Limitations

Some of the most valuable aspects of a business are NOT reflected on the balance sheet:
- Intellectual property
- Brand value
- Proprietary practices and processes
- Team talent and culture

## Statement 3: The Cash Flow Statement

### What it is

The cash flow statement shows where cash comes from, where it goes, and how much you have on hand. It tracks the actual movement of money in and out of the business.

**Core question it answers:** "Can I pay my bills?"

### Critical distinction: Profit vs. Cash

Profit and cash are fundamentally different:
- **Profit** = what is left over after all expenses (from the P&L)
- **Cash flow** = how much money is actually available at any given time

A company can be profitable on paper but still run out of cash. Remember the rule: **DRUM -- Don't Run Out of Money.** Even if you are making profits, running out of cash puts your business in jeopardy.

### Three categories of cash flow

| Category | What It Covers | Example |
|----------|---------------|---------|
| **Operating Activities** | Cash from core business operations | Selling ice cream, collecting payments from customers |
| **Investing Activities** | Cash spent on or received from long-term assets | Buying equipment, acquiring another company |
| **Financing Activities** | Cash from or to investors and lenders | Selling equity, taking loans, paying dividends |

### How it connects to other statements

- Net income from the P&L appears at the top of the cash flow statement
- Cash and cash equivalents from the cash flow statement appear on the balance sheet
- The three statements are deeply interrelated

### How to read a cash flow statement

The key question: **Is your cash flow positive or negative?**

**Real-world example -- Amazon:**
- Operating activities: +$66 billion in cash generated
- Investing activities: -$60 billion spent on investments
- Financing activities: -$1.1 billion in financing costs

For startups, the cash flow statement is often the most important statement because it tells you your runway -- how long until you need to raise more money.

## Key Financial Metrics and Ratios

### From the Income Statement

| Metric | Formula | What It Tells You |
|--------|---------|-------------------|
| Gross Margin % | (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue x 100 | How efficiently you produce goods/services |
| Operating Margin % | EBITDA / Revenue x 100 | How efficiently you run the business |
| Net Profit Margin % | Net Income / Revenue x 100 | Overall profitability after all costs |

### From the Balance Sheet

| Metric | Formula | What It Tells You |
|--------|---------|-------------------|
| Debt-to-Equity Ratio | Total Liabilities / Shareholder Equity | How leveraged (indebted) the company is |
| Current Ratio | Current Assets / Current Liabilities | Ability to pay short-term obligations |

### From the Cash Flow Statement

| Metric | What It Tells You |
|--------|-------------------|
| Free Cash Flow | Cash from operations minus capital expenditures -- what is available for growth, dividends, or debt repayment |
| Cash Runway (startups) | Cash on hand / monthly burn rate -- how many months you can operate before needing new funding |

## How the Three Statements Work Together

| Statement | Time Frame | Core Question | Key Insight |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| Income Statement (P&L) | Period (quarter/year) | Is my business profitable? | Revenue vs. expenses over time |
| Balance Sheet | Point in time (snapshot) | Am I solvent? | What I own vs. what I owe right now |
| Cash Flow Statement | Period (quarter/year) | Can I pay my bills? | Actual cash movement in and out |

They answer specifically different questions, but they are all absolutely interrelated. You need all three to understand a business.

## Financial Fluency Methodology: A Practical Checklist

When you sit down with any financial statement:

1. **Identify it.** Is this a P&L, balance sheet, or cash flow statement?
2. **Spot key terms.** Find revenue, COGS, net income (P&L); assets, liabilities, equity (balance sheet); operating, investing, financing (cash flow)
3. **Anchor to big numbers.** Find the largest figure and orient yourself around it
4. **Analyze vertically.** What are the proportions within this period?
5. **Analyze horizontally.** How have things changed over time?
6. **Benchmark.** How does this compare to competitors or industry averages?
7. **Draw conclusions.** What story do the numbers tell? What actions should follow?
8. **Remember:** Financial statements are narrations and interpretations, not pure facts. The numbers must be grounded in truth, but many different stories can be told from the same data.

## Important Caveats

- **Accounting standards vary by country.** The statements discussed here follow US Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP). Rules about when revenue is recognized (when earned vs. when paid), for example, differ internationally.
- **International transactions require extra scrutiny.** If your company is acquiring or being acquired by a foreign company, pay close attention to differences in accounting treatment.
- **Not every income statement has every line item.** Every business is different. Some P&Ls will have items that others do not.
- **Financial statements are not predictive analytics.** They are backward-looking records with forward-looking implications, but they require human interpretation.

## Recession-Proofing Strategies

While the full Recession-Proofing course content was unavailable in text form, the Financial Fluency courses address recession preparedness through several practical lenses:

### Cash sensitivity in uncertain times

- Focus on the cash flow statement above all else during economic downturns
- Know your burn rate and runway at all times
- Apply DRUM (Don't Run Out of Money) as a guiding principle

### Using financial fluency defensively

- **Cost control:** Use P&L analysis to identify where expenses can be reduced without destroying value
- **Budget advocacy:** Use financial data to make the case for your department's budget, rather than accepting across-the-board cuts
- **Strategic planning:** Use balance sheet analysis to understand leverage and debt exposure before downturns hit
- **Competitive positioning:** Use benchmarking to identify where you are more or less efficient than competitors and act accordingly

### Building resilience

- Maintain positive cash flow as the first priority
- Build reserves (visible on the balance sheet as cash and cash equivalents)
- Understand the difference between cutting costs intelligently (reducing SG&A on non-essential items) and damaging the business (cutting COGS corners that hurt quality)
- Use financial fluency to "not only make the tough decisions but maybe try and combat some of the tough ones and make your own case"

## Quick Reference: Financial Statement Cheat Sheet

**P&L tells you:** Revenue, costs, profit, efficiency, competitiveness
- Key line: Net Income (bottom line)
- Key ratios: Gross margin, operating margin, net margin

**Balance Sheet tells you:** Solvency, leverage, what you own vs. owe
- Key equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity
- Key ratios: Current ratio, debt-to-equity

**Cash Flow tells you:** Liquidity, runway, ability to pay bills
- Key sections: Operating, investing, financing
- Key metric: Is cash flow positive or negative?

**The SpeeD-Up method:** Spot terms, then Draw conclusions (up/down for vertical, left/right for horizontal analysis)
