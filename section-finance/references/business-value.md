# Building & Proving Business Value Reference

A synthesized guide to building business cases, proving financial impact, and evaluating opportunities with an investor mindset. Drawn from Section School's Proving Business Value Sprint (Nicole Alexander, Meta/NYU), Building a Business Case, and Investor Mindset Sprint (Eric Kim, Goodwater Capital).

---

## Part 1: Building a Business Case

### What is a business case?

A business case is a compelling story that presents an opportunity to add value to an organization and inspires others to act. Think of it like a legal case: you gather evidence, build an argument, prepare for counter-arguments, and persuade decision-makers.

### Who writes business cases?

Anyone -- from individual contributors to C-suite executives. The scope and rigor differ:

| Type | Authors | Audience | Examples |
|------|---------|----------|----------|
| Large, cross-functional initiatives (more rigor) | Executives | Leadership team, VC, board | New product launch, new org structure, enterprise GTM strategy, pricing strategy change |
| In-function initiatives (less rigor) | ICs to VPs | Manager, functional VP, some leaders | New marketing channel test, performance review change, feature removal, new supplier terms, adding an inbound sales team |

### Three characteristics of business cases that get greenlit

1. **Align with company strategy.** Are you in growth or cost-cutting mode? Check your company's three-year plan, department memos, or team roadmap.
2. **Grounded in data and evidence.** Don't rely on instinct alone. Use company data, market research, and subject matter interviews.
3. **Drive positive impact.** In the for-profit world: make money or save money. For NGOs/nonprofits: progress against mission.

## The Four-Component Framework

Every business case has four components, regardless of size or complexity:

### Component 1: The Idea

**Purpose:** Explain how your idea will provide value. Like a lawyer's opening statement, capture attention by linking your idea to a real problem or opportunity.

**Two key elements:**

**A. State the problem or opportunity**
Identify what you want to address. Be specific.

**B. Develop your hypothesis**
Follow these steps to validate that your hypothesis merits a full business case:

1. **Draft it.** Get your initial idea on paper. Be willing to pivot. (Example: Adidas team pivoted from apparel rental to equipment rental after finding the apparel market was oversaturated.)

2. **Check strategic alignment.** If your idea doesn't align with company priorities, your audience won't buy in. If Adidas were in cost-cutting mode, a new service launch wouldn't fly.

3. **Prove demand exists.** Quantify the underlying market. For a large company, show market size in the millions. Segment your target audience. (Example: Urban occasional athletes + environmentally conscious consumers + people trying new sports = ~40 million addressable market.)

4. **Size the impact.** Estimate what your idea will generate or save once fully rolled out. Back-of-napkin math is fine at this stage.
   - Formula: Addressable Market x Capture Rate x Average Revenue per Customer
   - Example: 40M x 5% = 2M customers x $50 avg revenue = $100M/year

5. **Share and get feedback.** Vet assumptions with finance, marketing, and subject matter experts. Be ready to revise. Your colleagues may reveal that your assumptions are too aggressive, or they may suggest a better approach entirely.

6. **Decide whether to proceed.** Is your organization capable of implementing this? Is the impact big enough? How confident are you in your assumptions?

**Key takeaways for the Idea stage:**
- Don't develop in a vacuum. Sharing makes ideas stronger and generates buy-in.
- Record everything you learn from stakeholders -- it saves time later and gives you ammunition for follow-up questions.
- Keep scope narrow. A clear, focused problem is easier to build a feasible plan around.

---

### Component 2: Proposed Implementation

**Purpose:** Prove you've done your homework. Show decision-makers you are both a strategic thinker and someone who can execute.

**Five elements:**

1. **High-level overview.** What are the top 3-5 things that explain how you'll make this happen? (Example: Leverage existing stores, daily rental fee model, pilot with 20 locations and 200K customers, then national rollout.)

2. **Detailed timeline.** Highlight key milestones. Use generic dates and build in buffer. Decision-makers prefer a realistic timeline you can meet over an aggressive one that needs extensions. Don't forget pre-launch activities.

3. **Assign owners.** You are the champion, but you won't execute alone. Map the cross-functional team needed. Determining owners also helps draft the initial budget.

4. **Initial budget.** Break it into phases. High-level is fine here since you'll dive deeper in the metrics section. (Example: $24M in year one, $88M over five years.)

5. **Bottom-line impact summary.** End the implementation section with the payoff. You've just asked for significant resources, so show why it's worth it. Include both short-term (which may be negative) and long-term projections.

**Key takeaways for Implementation:**
- Find an executive sponsor or champion early
- Share drafts before the formal presentation to generate buy-in
- Map timeline and budget conservatively. Over-deliver rather than ask for extensions.

### Component 3: Success Metrics

**Purpose:** Prove your case is a smart financial decision using measurable criteria.

#### Step 1: Define KPIs

Key Performance Indicators are the data points that tell you if the project is on or off track.

Example KPIs for a new service:
- New customers per month (adoption)
- Average revenue per customer and total revenue (financial performance)
- Customer satisfaction score (quality)

#### Step 2: Determine tracking cadence

- How often will you review KPIs?
- Who is part of the conversation?
- When do you escalate to leadership?
- Example: Weekly review with core team, quarterly report to leadership, immediate escalation if 25% below targets.

#### Step 3: Basic Financial Projections

**The business growth engine:** Money goes in (cost) and money comes out (revenue). If revenue exceeds cost, you have profit.

| Metric | Definition | Formula | When to Use |
|--------|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **Project Costs** | One-off upfront expenses (equipment, initial marketing, training) | Sum of all initial investments | Every business case |
| **Operating Costs** | Recurring expenses to maintain the initiative (rent, COGS, labor) | Sum of ongoing annual expenses | Every business case |
| **Revenue** | Money your idea generates | Customers x Average Revenue per Customer | Every business case |
| **ROI** | How profitable the investment is, as a percentage | (Net Profit / Total Cost) x 100 | Comparing financial health of different initiatives |
| **Breakeven** | How many units you need to sell to cover all costs | Fixed Costs / (Price per Unit - Variable Cost per Unit) | Thinking through pricing for new products/services |

**ROI example:** Year 1 costs = $24M, Year 1 revenue = $8M, ROI = negative. Year 5 cumulative: ROI = 400%+ (for every $1 invested, $4 in profit).

**Breakeven example:** Fixed costs = $16M, average revenue per rental = $20, variable cost = $3 per rental. Breakeven = $16M / ($20 - $3) = ~941,000 rentals.

#### Step 4: Advanced Financial Projections

To tell a more nuanced story, identify which "growth knob" your initiative turns:

**Knob 1: Efficiency (doing more with less)**

| Metric | Definition | Use Case |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Cost reduction | Decrease fixed or variable costs | Process improvement, automation |
| Throughput time | Total time to run a process start-to-finish | Manufacturing, software development |

**Knob 2: Market Share (capturing more customers)**

| Metric | Definition | Formula |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| ROAS (Return on Ad Spend) | ROI for a specific campaign | Campaign Revenue / Campaign Cost |
| CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) | Cost to obtain one new customer | Total Marketing & Sales Spend / New Customers Acquired |

**Knob 3: Customer Satisfaction (creating more value)**

| Metric | Definition | Formula |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| DAU/MAU | Daily/Monthly Active Users | Count of unique users in period |
| CLV (Customer Lifetime Value) | Total revenue expected from one customer over their lifecycle | Avg Revenue per Year x Average Customer Lifespan |

**Metric selection guidance:** Understand which knob you're turning, then select metrics accordingly:
- Efficiency projects: focus on costs, productivity, ROI
- Market share projects: add revenue metrics, CAC, ROAS
- Customer satisfaction projects: add engagement and long-term value metrics (DAU/MAU, CLV)

**Natural progression:** Early-stage initiatives (awareness) will have higher CAC and lower ROAS. Demonstrate a clear path to improving these over time.

#### Additional financial analysis tools

| Metric | What It Does | When to Use |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Payback Period** | How long until cumulative profits equal the initial investment | Comparing timeline risk across projects |
| **IRR (Internal Rate of Return)** | The annualized rate of return an investment generates | Comparing multiple project pitches. Tells investors which project has the highest rate of return. Does not indicate size of opportunity. |

### Component 4: Mitigating Risk

**Purpose:** Anticipate what could go wrong and show how you'll handle it. This demonstrates leadership and builds trust -- it does NOT make your case look weaker.

#### Risks vs. Dependencies

- **Risks:** Events that may happen and could be detrimental (market projections don't materialize, quality issues, brand damage)
- **Dependencies:** Things you rely on others to make happen (storage capacity in pilot stores, staff training completion, partner cooperation)

#### Common risk patterns by growth knob

**Efficiency projects:**
- Streamlining inadvertently reduces product/service quality
- More automation causes congestion or new bottlenecks
- Resistance to change from staff causes delays or low adoption

**Market share projects:**
- Channel risk: no reliable path to reach new customers at scale
- Inefficient monetization: high acquisition costs with unclear path to positive ROI
- Need social strategy, e-commerce, or new distribution channels

**Customer satisfaction projects:**
- Customers don't respond as anticipated
- Initiative doesn't actually create more value
- Brand erosion: differentiating your offering hurts existing brand positioning

**Universal risks (any project):**
- Budget overruns
- Macroeconomic changes affecting demand
- Timeline slippage

#### Risk mitigation process

1. Go through your KPIs line by line
2. Ask: "What risks and dependencies could alter this KPI?"
3. Ask: "How would that change the profitability of my idea?"
4. For each identified risk, document a specific mitigation plan
5. Examples of mitigations: customer surveys, A/B testing, pilot programs, quality control processes, modular training, weekly standups, buffer time in timelines

## Presenting Your Business Case

### Slide structure (aim for 8-10 slides total)

| Slide | Content |
|-------|---------|
| 1. Executive Summary | Problem, hypothesis, expected impact, and one key risk mitigation (build this LAST) |
| 2. Problem | The issue you're addressing, backed by data and a direct quote or stat |
| 3. Hypothesis/Proposal | Your proposal + top 3 benefits your audience cares most about |
| 4. Implementation: Timeline & Owners | Key milestones, cross-functional team, confirmation of buy-in |
| 5. Implementation: Budget & Impact | Cost breakdown, short-term and long-term ROI |
| 6. Success Metrics: KPIs & Basic Financials | KPIs with baselines and goals, ROI, breakeven |
| 7. Success Metrics: Advanced Financials | ROAS, CAC, CLV, or efficiency metrics as applicable |
| 8. Risks, Dependencies & Mitigations | Most critical risks and your plan for each |
| 9. Next Steps | What happens if greenlit |

### Presentation principles

1. **Keep your audience top of mind.** What matters most to them? What objections can you address up front? What do they typically worry about?
2. **Provide baselines and goals** for every metric. Don't just say "we'll track ROAS" -- show the current state and your target.
3. **It's okay not to have all the answers.** If someone raises a risk you hadn't considered, commit to follow up. Transparency builds trust.
4. **Tailor to known preferences.** If your CEO is risk-averse, address risk on slide 1. If your audience is numbers-driven, lead with financial impact.
5. **Don't analyze to death.** Get to 80% and present. Great business case authors balance rigor with execution.

### Case Study: Axios HQ

Axios proved business value for its HQ software product using the four-part framework:

1. **Idea:** Corporate communication is time-consuming. Smart Brevity (Axios' copyrighted writing technique) could help. Evidence: Axios email open rate was 48%, double the industry average.
2. **Implementation:** Hired engineering, enterprise sales, and customer success teams. Set milestones around first paying customers. Started as a division of Axios Media.
3. **Success Metrics:** Recurring revenue ($3M year 1, projecting $7M year 2, ~10% of total revenue). 200+ customers in year 1. 100% renewal rate from Q1 cohort.
4. **Risk Mitigation:** Began with a pilot program to limit financial exposure before full buildout. After proving value, Axios HQ separated and raised $20M Series A.

**Lessons from Axios HQ:**
- Build a proof of concept. Low-lift ways to prove an idea on a small scale add credibility.
- Align with strategic priorities. Every media business needs new revenue streams. Axios HQ emphasized recurring revenue.

## Part 2: The Investor Mindset

### Core philosophy

Every decision you make at work -- scheduling a meeting, proposing an initiative, allocating budget -- is a financial investment. Effective leaders think like investors: they evaluate risk and reward systematically to make sound decisions and spark meaningful growth.

### The Risk/Reward Framework

Six components, worked through sequentially:

```
1. Thesis (the big bet)
     |
2. Key Drivers (strategic levers)
     |
3. Key Metrics (quantified tracking)
     |
4. Key Risks (barriers and threats)
     |
5. Underwriting (research to price each risk)
     |
6. Leap of Faith (explicit remaining unknowns)
     |
   = CONVICTION (informed decision to act)
```

### Component 1: The Thesis

The thesis articulates your big bet on the company's (or initiative's) future. What will it look like if it achieves massive growth? Think 5-10 years ahead, when operating at best capacity.

**Three qualities of a strong thesis:**

| Quality | Definition | Snap Example |
|---------|-----------|--------------|
| **Aspirational** | Think big, be audacious | "Snap will be the best digital community for Gen Z" |
| **Measurable** | You can look back and determine if it held true | "Leading to greater than $10B in ad revenue by 2027" |
| **Achievable** | Within the limits of time, law, and physics | Achievable because US digital ad spend forecast >$300B |

**How to develop a thesis:**
1. What are the company's future revenue and profit projections?
2. What is the market opportunity based on the industry's 5-10 year outlook?
3. Given these, if you were extremely aggressive, how much could you grow?
4. Stay focused on the strategic rationale. What unique advantage enables this?

**Example theses:**
- Snap: "Snap will be the best digital community for Gen Z consumers, becoming the preferred advertising platform and generating >$10B in revenue by 2027."
- Airbnb: "Airbnb will be the largest provider of nightly stays in the world without owning a single hotel room."

**Key takeaways:**
- Don't take shortcuts when writing a thesis
- Use market sizes and trends to think big -- swing for the fences
- Transcend quarterly KPIs and annual reports

---

### Component 2: Key Drivers

Key drivers are the strategic levers that, if true, allow your thesis to become reality. This is the "artist" phase -- imaginative, strategic thinking. Jumping straight to metrics is a major pitfall.

**Process:**

1. **Deconstruct the thesis into components.** (Snap: best digital community + preferred ad platform + >$10B revenue)

2. **Brainstorm strategic levers for each component.** Generate as many as possible.
   - Best digital community: network effects, world-class product org, relevant authentic content
   - Preferred ad platform: high ROAS for advertisers, creative freedom, cost-effective branded features
   - >$10B revenue: shifts in ad spending, new product innovation

3. **Prioritize 2-4 key drivers.** Which are absolutely necessary? Which already have traction?
   - Look for clues in existing metrics
   - Ask: "If I could only pick one thing that makes or breaks this thesis, what would it be?"

**Key takeaways:**
- Writer's block? Imagine what a competitor with unlimited resources would do to win.
- Revisit key drivers as your industry and customer behaviors evolve.
- Read investor memoranda to understand how professional investors articulate their conviction.

---

### Component 3: Key Metrics

Key metrics are customer-centric measures that track your key drivers. Financial measures are the baseline, but focus on the underlying metrics that move them.

**Process:**

1. **Categorize each key driver** into growth, engagement, or monetization
2. **Select metrics that directly connect** customer behavior to key drivers:
   - Network effects: New User Growth, Organic vs. Paid Growth
   - Product excellence: Retention Rates
   - Advertising ROI: Average Revenue per User (ARPU)

3. **Benchmark against competitors** -- specifically at the same growth stage
   - Early Snap benchmarks against early-stage Facebook, not current Facebook
   - Be product-specific: compare like-for-like (Wish vs. Best Buy, not Walmart)

**Key takeaways:**
- Track key metrics over time. If growth is trending upward but key metrics aren't, you may have the wrong metrics.
- Analyze by cohorts (e.g., January sign-ups vs. June sign-ups) to see if you're improving the product.
- Well-chosen key metrics build better financial forecasts.

---

### Component 4: Key Risks

Now become the skeptic. Key risks are the barriers to your thesis -- what would threaten your drivers and sink your metrics?

**Use Porter's Five Forces as a generator:**

1. **Industry competition** -- existing rivals
2. **New players** -- emerging threats
3. **Supplier power** -- dependency on key suppliers
4. **Buyer power** -- customer leverage
5. **Threat of substitutes** -- alternative products/services

**For each key driver, identify at least two risks:**

| Key Driver | Risk Examples (Snap) |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Network Effects | Decline in user growth; older generations joining dilutes Gen Z appeal; intrusive ads |
| Product Innovation | Global competition (Instagram copied Stories); losing consumers to other platforms; lack of funding for innovation |
| Advertising ROI | Overestimating ad effectiveness; macro slowdown reduces ad spending |

**Where to research risks:**
- Annual reports, quarterly updates, S-1 filings
- Financial commitments (e.g., Snap owed Google Cloud and AWS $3B)
- Competitor analysis

**Key takeaways:**
- Identify at least two risks per key driver
- The better you articulate each risk, the easier it is to research and underwrite it
- Going deep on key risks serves as a trigger for action, not paralysis

---

### Component 5: Underwriting Risk

Underwriting is the process of determining how risky a venture is and whether those risks are acceptable. The term originates from the 1600s, when insurers would write their names underneath how much risk they were willing to assume. In an investment context, it means conducting due diligence.

**Process: Create a research plan for each risk.**

| Risk | Research Actions |
|------|-----------------|
| Declining user growth | Survey users: likelihood of continued use, what they love, what they'd improve |
| Increasing competition | Competitor product benchmarking; track whether Gen Z is shifting to other platforms |
| Monetization effectiveness | Consult Fortune 500 companies and ad agencies about Snap's effectiveness from a marketer's perspective |

**The underwriting process may involve:**
- Reviewing financial statements
- Assessing market conditions
- Evaluating management teams
- Analyzing competition, regulatory changes, economic conditions
- Consulting industry experts

As you do more underwriting and diligence, the leap of faith diminishes -- but never totally disappears.

---

### Component 6: Leap of Faith

Make explicit any remaining unknowns you cannot underwrite. This is not closing your eyes and jumping. It is a conscious, informed acknowledgment of what you cannot know.

- Some risks cannot be researched (you lack the resources, or the information is unknowable)
- External shocks are always possible (e.g., Musical.ly merged with ByteDance unexpectedly; the same could happen to any company)
- The goal is to minimize the leap through rigorous underwriting, then accept the remaining uncertainty with clear eyes

---

### From Analysis to Conviction

**The outcome of the investor mindset process is conviction** -- an informed, evidence-based confidence in your thesis.

**Quantify conviction:**
- Investors set a price target on their investments
- Teams can vote 1-4 (no middle scores) on conviction level after completing the process
- This forces people to commit to a position

**The complete flow:**
1. Think big (thesis)
2. Think like an artist (key drivers)
3. Think like an analyst (key metrics)
4. Think like a skeptic (key risks)
5. Do the homework (underwriting)
6. Accept what you can't know (leap of faith)
7. Act with conviction

**Final principle:** Thinking like an investor makes you a better operator. Whether you are investing capital, proposing an initiative, or evaluating a strategic decision, this systematic approach to risk and reward produces better outcomes.

## Quick Reference: Business Case Checklist

- [ ] Problem or opportunity clearly stated
- [ ] Hypothesis developed and vetted with stakeholders
- [ ] Strategic alignment confirmed
- [ ] Demand validated with data
- [ ] Impact sized (back-of-napkin is fine initially)
- [ ] High-level implementation plan with timeline, owners, budget
- [ ] KPIs defined with tracking cadence and escalation criteria
- [ ] Basic financials calculated (project costs, operating costs, revenue, ROI, breakeven)
- [ ] Advanced financials as needed (ROAS, CAC, CLV, throughput, IRR)
- [ ] Risks and dependencies identified with mitigations
- [ ] Executive summary written (last)
- [ ] Presentation tailored to audience priorities
- [ ] Baselines and goals provided for every metric
