# Innovation Strategy Frameworks

Reference document synthesized from the "Innovation Strategy Sprint" (taught by Professor Sara Beckman, UC Berkeley Haas School of Business). Covers the Innovation Cycle, Growth Opportunity Framework, Jobs-to-Be-Done, Web of Abstraction, Journey Audit, Competency Audit, RIME Framework, and Testing.

---

## Core Philosophy

Innovation is not about random creativity -- it is a repeatable, systematic process grounded in deep customer understanding. The Innovation Cycle provides four capabilities that enable you to develop new customer experiences. Focus on experiences, not just products, because experiences create more value for customers, are harder to copy, increase loyalty, and strengthen your brand.

**Three learning outcomes:**
1. Discover hidden growth opportunities for your business
2. Develop a fresh way to think about framing and solving customer problems
3. Apply frameworks and models to better understand customers, prioritize ideas, and de-risk new concepts

---

## The Innovation Cycle

A four-phase iterative process for systematic innovation:

### Phase 1: Understand
Observe your customers' behaviors to grasp their problems. Get out of the building. Operate in the concrete world of direct observation.

### Phase 2: Reframe
Move into the abstract, intellectual space. Reframe your customer's problem based on your understanding. This is "sense-making" -- the step many companies skip because they rush to build solutions.

> "If I had an hour to solve a problem, I'd spend 55 minutes thinking about the problem and 5 minutes thinking about solutions." -- attributed to Einstein

### Phase 3: Design
Cross back into the concrete world to design solutions in response to the reframed problem. Develop different offerings, solutions, and potential experiences.

### Phase 4: Test
Test your solution to gain a better understanding of how it works. Observe customer reactions. Update your mental models based on evidence.

**The cycle repeats:** Reframe -> Design -> Test -> Understand -> Repeat

**Critical reminder:** The Innovation Cycle is highly iterative. Performing just one activity is not sufficient. Develop these capabilities and return to the process again and again.

**Key insight on divergence and convergence:** In meetings, separate diverging (generating ideas) from converging (evaluating ideas). If someone proposes an idea and the immediate response is "that won't work because..." you are converging too early. Generate ideas first, then evaluate.

## The Growth Opportunity Framework

Maps how businesses can grow along two dimensions:
- **X axis:** Developing new experiences
- **Y axis:** Developing new markets

### Three Classifications (with escalating risk)

1. **Improve (Low Risk):** Optimize existing offerings for existing customers. Incremental improvements to what you already do.

2. **Expand (Medium Risk):** Draw on existing capabilities to create new experiences for existing customers, OR take existing experiences to new markets.

3. **Diversify (High Risk):** Develop breakthroughs -- inventing new experiences for markets your company does not yet serve.

**Key principle:** Experiences create more value than products alone. Companies that create experiences have a competitive advantage by delivering on a more complete set of customer needs, which makes experiences more difficult to copy.

**Reflection questions:**
- What products, services, and experiences has your company launched recently? Where do they plot on the framework?
- Has your firm thought about the power of experiences? How has (or hasn't) that thinking helped the business?

## Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD) Framework

A mindset that puts customers at the center of innovation. JTBD helps develop a deep understanding of your customers' needs, which forms the basis for customer-centered innovation.

### Core Concept
"Why do I buy a drill? Not because I want a 1/4 inch drill -- I want a 1/4 inch hole. Why do I want a hole? I want to hang something on my wall. Why do I want to hang something? I want my room to be nice."

Jobs-to-Be-Done captures what the customer wants to accomplish -- their objectives, tasks, problems, needs, and aspirations.

### Three Rules for Identifying JTBD

1. **Jobs are about the customer, not about products or the company.** PMs commonly describe features of their products when asked about JTBD. Instead, describe what the customer is trying to get done.

2. **Describe individual jobs -- avoid grouping them.** A milkshake fulfills the need for food AND keeps someone company on a car ride. These are separate jobs. Separating them allows creative recombination.

3. **Make JTBD measurable.** Keep the outcome top of mind. By describing outcomes, you find ways to measure success. Example: "Reduce the time people need to get up to speed on QuickBooks."

### Three Types of Jobs
- **Functional:** Tasks and practical problems (get to work, process transactions)
- **Social:** How the customer wants to be perceived (look professional, feel included)
- **Emotional:** How the customer wants to feel (confident, secure, entertained)

### Using JTBD for Growth

**To explore new markets with existing experiences:**
1. What other JTBD can you address with an existing experience?
2. Do you observe customers using your product for different JTBD?
3. Can you expand your experience to other geographies with similar JTBD?

Example: Arm & Hammer discovered baking soda served jobs beyond baking (deodorizing, cleaning). Today 45% of revenue comes from personal care products.

**To develop new experiences:**
1. What JTBD are you not yet addressing?
2. Are your customers' JTBD changing?
3. Do you need to react to external changes impacting customers' JTBD?

Example: YETI expanded from insulated coolers (food storage job) to chairs (relaxation job) for the same camping customer.

### Organizational Value of JTBD

1. **Share JTBD throughout the organization.** Don't let PMs be the sole holders of customer knowledge.
2. **Use JTBD as a common unit for measuring success.** Anchoring around JTBD aligns the entire organization.
3. **JTBD can be valid for years.** Customer jobs outlast the technology used to support them, providing stability even as technology changes rapidly.

### How to Discover JTBD: Customer Interviews
- Do NOT open with "Wouldn't you love my product?"
- Ask open-ended questions
- Get customers to tell stories about the problem space
- Listen for the jobs behind the behaviors

## Web of Abstraction

A tool for reframing (Phase 2 of the Innovation Cycle) that turns raw JTBD insights into actionable innovation directions.

### How It Works

Start with a customer's job-to-be-done at the center.

**Ladder Up:** Move from the job toward associated outcomes.
- "Why does the customer want to do this job?"
- Uncover the functional, social, and emotional outcomes behind the primary job
- Reveals the higher-order needs the customer is trying to satisfy

**Ladder Down:** Move from the job toward obstacles.
- "What is standing in the way of the customer accomplishing this job?"
- Identifies barriers, friction points, and pain points
- Opens the door to opportunities for innovation

### Application Process
1. Identify a customer job-to-be-done
2. Ladder up: What outcomes are they trying to achieve?
3. Ladder down: What obstacles stand in their way?
4. Converge: Select which JTBD to address based on impact and feasibility
5. Use selected JTBD as the foundation for designing solutions

**Broader application:** The Web of Abstraction works for internal projects too. "My boss gave me a project" -- the project is the job-to-be-done. Step back: Why does my boss want this? What outcomes is she trying to achieve? What barriers stand in the way? This level of abstraction clarifies how to approach any assignment.

## Journey Audit

Identify gaps in your customers' journey that represent opportunities for innovation.

### Customer-Centric Journey Phases

Instead of the firm-centric view (pre-purchase, purchase, post-purchase), use the customer-centric lens:

1. **Discovery:** How does the customer become aware of and understand the solution?
2. **Action:** How does the customer acquire and begin using the solution?
3. **Benefit:** How does the customer derive ongoing value? (Include retention)

**Key questions:**
- Which phases do you currently address best?
- How can you better address the weaker phases?
- What gaps exist in your customers' current experience?

**Level up:** Consider the cyclical nature of customer journeys. The Benefit phase should loop back to Discovery through retention practices.

## Competency Audit

Take inventory of your company's existing assets and capabilities to find repurposing opportunities.

### Process
1. List your company's core competencies
2. Split them into **assets** (what you have: data, infrastructure, IP, brand) and **capabilities** (what you can do: engineering, design, distribution)
3. Investigate how existing competencies can be repurposed to design new experiences or develop new markets

**Key insight:** The Competency Audit can also identify moats -- areas where you could focus on building and sustaining competitive advantages.

## RIME Framework (Prioritization)

Use RIME to assess and prioritize which growth opportunities to pursue. RIME replaces gut feeling and "hippo" (highest paid person's opinion) decision-making with structured analysis.

### The Four Variables

**R - Reach:** How many customers or users will you be able to reach?
- Understand addressable market size
- Example: Peloton chose treadmills over rowing machines because the treadmill market was substantially larger

**I - Impact:** How well does the idea complete customers' jobs to be done?
- Aim to maximize customer satisfaction
- Use time spent, effort, and usage rates as proxies when direct measurement is difficult
- Score as high, medium, or low
- Example: Chime combined debit and credit card features to serve millennials' need to build credit while using preferred payment methods

**M - Monetization:** What would customers be willing to pay?
- Estimate customer willingness to pay using market data and competitor pricing
- Numbers don't need to be perfect but should be grounded in research
- Example: Before launch, Uber estimated willingness to pay based on the existing $9B cab market

**E - Effort:** What resources are required (time, people, expenditure)?
- Threshold for low/medium/high differs by company
- Example: Dyson's $70M hair dryer was low effort relative to its $500M electric car project

### Applying RIME: Uber Case Study

| Initiative | Reach | Impact | Monetization | Effort | Outcome |
|-----------|-------|--------|-------------|--------|---------|
| Uber Pool | High (expanded TAM) | High (price-sensitive riders) | Medium (20% less per ride) | Low (existing platform) | Success |
| Uber Eats | High (8.1M+ market) | High (grew in pandemic) | Medium ($61/user proxy) | Low (existing platform) | Success |
| Uber China | High (240M users) | Low (JTBD already served) | Medium ($65/user) | Very High (regulatory) | Failed ($1.5B loss) |

### Applying RIME: Atlassian Case Study

Atlassian's decision to pivot from IT services to Jira:
- **Reach:** 700K software developers in US with 95% expected growth. Developers work across teams, making them "pollinators" of the product.
- **Impact:** Founders could quantify efficiency gains before release -- they built it to solve their own problem.
- **Monetization:** Jira at $800/server vs. Microsoft Project at $999/user. Massive cost advantage.
- **Effort:** Built with $10K of credit card debt in 3 months by repurposing internal tools. Free trial model lowered acquisition costs 2x vs. peers.

**Result:** Cash flow positive after one year. No VC for first eight years. Now top-20 software company globally.

### Key RIME Takeaways
1. **Focus matters** -- you don't have unlimited capital, time, or people
2. **Include multiple stakeholders** to improve estimates and create organizational ownership
3. **Compare similarly sized ideas** -- mismatched scope makes comparison meaningless
4. **Continue collaborating after initial assessment** -- partnerships can improve any RIME variable
5. **RIME is not one-and-done** -- apply it continuously as conditions change

### Enhancing RIME Scores Through Partnerships
- Pottery Barn + Sherwin-Williams: helped customers pair paint and furniture (increased Impact, reduced Effort)
- Nike + Apple: Apple Watch Nike Plus (Nike got data capability, Apple got athlete demographic)

## Four Steps of Testing (Phase 4)

### The Process
1. **Extract hypotheses** from your analysis and RIME prioritization
2. **Design and run experiments** -- start cheap and quick when risk is high
3. **Analyze evidence** -- what did the experiment reveal?
4. **Decide on next steps:**
   - **Pivot:** Change direction based on what you learned
   - **Kill:** Abandon the idea (this is a valid and valuable outcome)
   - **Persevere:** Continue with increased investment and higher-fidelity experiments

**Key principle:** At the beginning of your innovation journey, when risk is high, start with cheap and quick experiments. As you reduce uncertainty through each iteration of the Innovation Cycle, increase the fidelity of your experiments.

## Quick-Reference: Innovation Process Checklist

- [ ] **Understand:** Observe customers, conduct interviews, identify JTBD
- [ ] **Reframe:** Use Web of Abstraction to ladder up (outcomes) and down (obstacles)
- [ ] **Map the journey:** Conduct Journey Audit (Discovery, Action, Benefit)
- [ ] **Inventory capabilities:** Conduct Competency Audit (assets and capabilities)
- [ ] **Prioritize:** Score opportunities using RIME (Reach, Impact, Monetization, Effort)
- [ ] **Test:** Extract hypotheses, design experiments, analyze evidence, decide (pivot/kill/persevere)
- [ ] **Repeat:** The Innovation Cycle is iterative -- keep cycling

## Key Takeaways

1. **Start with the customer, not the product.** Every framework in the Innovation Cycle is customer-centric.

2. **Spend more time on the problem than the solution.** The Reframe phase (sense-making) is where most companies cut corners, and where the greatest value lies.

3. **Experiences beat products.** They create more value, are harder to copy, and build loyalty.

4. **JTBD outlast technology.** Anchor your strategy around customer jobs, not technical solutions.

5. **Use RIME to replace gut feeling.** Structured prioritization prevents expensive mistakes like Uber China.

6. **Test cheaply first.** De-risk through iteration, not through big bets.
