# Leadership Frameworks Reference

Synthesized from courses: Complete Manager Sprint (Michael Bungay Stanier), Inclusive Leadership (Mita Mallick), and Leading Through Change (Peter Wang).

---

## Part 1: The Complete Manager Equation

**Core Formula:** Strong Relationships x Worthy Goals x Coaching = Complete Manager

Each component is a multiplier. A zero in any dimension destroys the final result:
- Goals + Coaching but no Trust = disconnected team, reluctant to collaborate or innovate
- Trust + Goals but no Coaching = handholding, no autonomy, people cannot self-develop
- Trust + Coaching but no Goals = supportive culture with no impact, development stalls

The manager's job: hit your targets, grow your people, enhance culture, and stay sane. Culture is half the equation (Satya Nadella devoted half to strategy, half to culture, shifting Microsoft from "know-it-all" to "learn-it-all," resulting in 700%+ market value growth).

## Part 2: Building Trust -- The Foundation

### The Three Cs of Trust

Trust is the baseline for everything else. Drawn from Google's Project Aristotle research on psychological safety, trust enables people to take risks without feeling insecure.

**1. Connection**
- Tied to empathy and closeness to others
- Understanding their interests, lives, priorities, and whole humanness
- Seeking to understand before being understood -- not just the challenge but the whole person
- Simple acts: birthday cards, thank-you notes, genuine check-ins

**2. Competence**
- Demonstrating ability where you are strong
- Being self-aware and open about where you lack expertise
- Being open to getting better and trusting people will help you

**3. Consistency**
- Reliability in what you do and how you show up
- Making commitments you can keep, then keeping them
- "Do what you say and say what you do"
- When things change, communicating clearly and soon
- Avoiding mixed signals (e.g., only positive feedback followed by a negative evaluation)

### Building Trust -- Practical Principles

1. **Choose to trust first.** Do not make people earn it. When trusted, people rise to the occasion. Make it safe to be human.
2. **Trust surfaces problems earlier.** Psychological safety means the team tells you about issues while they are still easy to address. Without trust, people hide problems until they are unavoidable.
3. **Lead with vulnerability.** Showing you are human gives permission for others to show up as their full selves. The strongest signal in the room is leading by example -- not perfect, not demanding, but vulnerable and consistent.
4. **Trust is woven continuously.** It is an ongoing process of strengthening through finding opportunities, taking risk, and delivering with confidence and consistency.
5. **Trust is not about perfection.** You will make mistakes and let people down. Being committed to trust creates a foundation for relationship resilience -- when something goes wrong, the relationship can be mended.

### Diagnostic Questions

Use the Three Cs to evaluate your most important work relationships:
- Where are you strong? Where are you lacking?
- In the last 24 hours, how did the 3 Cs show up?
- What are you doing that might be building trust? Diminishing it?

## Part 3: The Operating Manual

A personal operating manual describes how you best work, who you are, and how that impacts your relationships. It makes the implicit explicit and reduces assumptions.

### Three Steps to Deploy Operating Manuals

**Step 1: Information (Know Thyself)**

Core questions for the operating manual:
1. What do people get right about you?
2. What do people underappreciate or underestimate about you?
3. What are 3 essential things about you?
4. In good relationships, what did you do, or not do?
5. In frustrating relationships, what did you do, or not do?
6. In stressful relationship times: what pushed your buttons? Which patterns of yours irritated others?

Additional questions to consider:
- How do you use certain forms of communication?
- How do you prefer to digest information (async vs. sync)?
- What makes a meeting successful?
- What behaviors do you value the most?
- How do you schedule time on your calendar?
- How do you like to run one-to-ones?
- What does good collaboration look like to you?
- How do you like to make decisions?

Three triangulation tests for each answer:
1. Degree of confidence: Low / Medium / High?
2. What data or examples support your response?
3. Share with someone you trust (the "Spouse-ish test") and revise based on their reaction.

**Step 2: Conversation**

Share operating manuals in a live conversation with each teammate. Both parties should aim for:
1. Mutual understanding of who each person is and what makes them happy/unhappy
2. Permission to keep checking in and trying to understand each other
3. Building the best possible working relationship, anticipating friction points

Before the conversation:
- Get in your best place mentally
- Consider changing the physical location to signal this is different
- Explain the purpose, note that hierarchies are not at play
- Acknowledge the shared awkwardness
- Both share openly and equally

Closing questions:
- "What was most helpful or valuable for you?"
- "What needs to be said that hasn't yet been said?"

**Step 3: Ongoing Maintenance**

- Commit to regularly checking in: daily, weekly, monthly
- Use two recurring questions: "How are things going with how you and I are working right now?"
- Update the manual as preferences change (it is a living document)
- Jay Desai (PatientPing/Bamboo Health) conducts monthly 1-hour 1:1s for this purpose

### Key Principles
- Complete all 3 steps. Do not skip the conversation or maintenance.
- Lean into the awkwardness. It will feel uncomfortable. That is the point.
- Prioritize operating manuals with new teammates.
- Share early -- Desai shares his during candidate interviews.
- Recognize workplace hierarchies and use the manual to clarify expectations up and down.

## Part 4: Worthy Goals

### Why Traditional Goal Frameworks Fall Short

- **SMART goals** narrow and tighten, but do not ensure the goal is right. "You can end up polishing a turd."
- **OKRs** help track business metrics but can become metrics-driven tail wagging the dog.
- **BHAGs** are aspirational but lack a personal connection framework.
- Example of goals gone wrong: Wells Fargo's aggressive targets led to fraud.

### The Worthy Goal Framework

A worthy goal captures deep personal motivation, connects to team and company priorities, and raises the bar on learning. It must be:

**1. Thrilling**
- Speaks to the person's strengths, values, and capabilities
- Test: "On a dark winter morning, would you get out of bed to work toward this goal?"
- Without thrill, people burn out when things get hard

**2. Important**
- Connects to the big picture -- department and company goals
- Test: Does it align with your team's north star metric or KPI?
- Without importance, the goal lacks strategic relevance

**3. Daunting**
- Challenges the person to develop and keep growing
- Stretch but doable (the Goldilocks zone)
- Without being daunting, people stay in their comfort zone, leading to complacency
- Daunting goals drive higher performance and evolution

### The Four-Step Drafting Process

**Step 1: The Crappy First Draft**
- Write a best-guess goal that is thrilling-ish, important-ish, daunting-ish
- "Just give me a v0.crap" (Courtney Hohne, X)
- Apply three tests:
  - **Spouse-ish test**: Share with someone close. Not seeking approval, seeking feedback on thrill.
  - **FOSO test** ("For the Sake Of"): Examines importance. "This goal is for the sake of ___." What broader impact does achieving it serve?
  - **Goldilocks zone**: Is it heavy enough? Light enough? Find the sweet spot of daunting.

**Step 2: The Action Draft**
- Add an actionable verb (begin, launch, create, produce, reinvent, dismantle, etc.)
- Research shows envisioning the action step is more psychologically motivating than just envisioning the outcome
- Score the goal 1-7 on Thrilling, Important, Daunting. Target 18/21 or above for "strong."

**Step 3: Turn Up the Heat**
- Choose one word that tightens or makes the goal more specific
- Consider six focus areas: Commitment, Reach, Time Limit, Scope, Standard, Outcome

**Step 4: Ready for Commitment**
- If it has passed through steps 1-3, it is ready. May not be perfect, but commit and continue to refine.

### Worthy Goals -- Key Principles
- Anchor to an existing business goal (team, department, company)
- Be patient. Focus on the process rather than rushing to produce a goal quickly.
- Role model it. Set worthy goals for yourself before helping teammates do the same.
- Facilitate with coaching questions rather than giving advice.
- Continue conversations about the goal even if failing. The process is iterative.
- Goals are more than distilled numbers and benchmarks.

### Worthy Goals at Scale: Lessons from X (Google Moonshots)

The X moonshot blueprint is a Venn diagram of:
1. Huge problem affecting millions
2. Breakthrough technology (5-10 years out)
3. Radical solution that could unleash 10x improvement

This maps directly to Thrilling, Important, Daunting. Key practices:
- Set long-term audacious visions as the north star, not concrete near-term goals
- Give awards for the audaciousness of the goal, not just the outcome
- Reward bigger thinking at the start; smaller, safer goals come later
- The goal is a target to accelerate learnings, not the point itself
- Psychological safety enables the risk-taking needed for worthy goals

## Part 5: Coaching -- Being Coach-Like

### The Mindset Shift

Coaching does not mean becoming a professional coach. It means "being coach-like" -- shifting from using your knowledge to solve problems to enabling the people around you to solve their own problems.

What worked as an individual contributor may not work as a manager. The transition requires moving from heavy cognitive lifting to inviting teammates to solve problems. Work less hard, have more impact.

Google's Project Aristotle found good coaching, empowering teammates, and building an inclusive team were the top three effectiveness factors. Driving productivity was number four.

Research shows 3 months of coaching increases resilience, confidence, and collaboration while reducing stress and anxiety. Employers see 3.5-5x ROI for retention.

### The Advice Monster

Your urge to tell people what to do, provide solutions, and add value. When it takes over:
- You silence your teammates
- You send a subliminal message: "You're not good enough. I'm the only one who can have the answer."
- You form a bottleneck. Instead of leading, you are doing the work.
- A 2021 Gallup poll found managers leading teams face high burnout partly from this pattern.

The antidote: be curious. Assume others have the capacity to teach you something. Instead of giving advice, ask questions.

### The Five Coaching Questions

**1. The Kickstart Question: "What's on your mind?"**
- Accelerates into the heart of the challenge
- Open-ended, inviting autonomy, empowerment, and control
- Then be quiet. Stay curious, no advice, just a little longer.
- Shift of power: "I trust you, I'm interested in you."

**2. The Focus Question: "What's the real challenge here for you?"**
- People often work hard to solve the wrong problem
- Helps teammates zero in on what is essential
- The phrase "for you" moves the spotlight from the problem to the person
- Inspires reflection, learning, and growth while figuring out the challenge

**3. The A.W.E. Question: "And what else?"**
- "Best coaching question in the world"
- Someone's first answer is never their only answer, and rarely their best
- Forces slowing down and listening more
- Can be asked multiple times. Often the real issue surfaces after the 5th or 7th ask.
- Builds on the Focus question to drive deeper thinking

**4. The Foundation Question: "What do you want?"**
- Keep in your back pocket for when conversations go in circles or seem dry
- Resets and goes deeper
- Tone matters: try "Just out of curiosity, what do you want here?"
- Empowers people to ask for what they need

**5. The Learning Question: "What was most valuable or useful here for you?"**
- Requires the teammate to recall, internalize, and articulate
- Brain creates new neural pathways
- Give time and space to reflect and respond, then offer your own response
- Reinforces commonality and finishes on a positive note
- Activates reflection and ensures the right takeaway

### Using the Questions in Practice

**Sequencing:** Open with the Kickstart question, use Focus and A.W.E. to go deeper during the conversation, deploy the Foundation question when needed, and close with the Learning question. This is not a rigid script -- weave questions naturally.

**Key practices:**
- Give your teammate time to share. Get comfortable with silence. Even 3 seconds of wait time can be enough.
- Explore question combinations. Use them face-to-face, in email, or on Slack.
- Tone matters. Sincerity is key. When you show genuine curiosity, it opens up.
- Your teammates will not notice what you are doing. They are too busy thinking of their answers.
- Coach-like interactions can be short. They do not need to be formal sessions.
- Let curiosity be your guide.

### The 3Ps Model for Coaching Focus

When a teammate brings a challenge, it typically falls into one of three domains:
- **Project**: The technical content of the situation
- **People**: The relationship dynamics involved
- **Patterns**: The recurring behaviors the person keeps running into

Use the Focus question to identify which domain is most relevant before diving deeper.

## Part 6: Operationalizing the Complete Manager Equation

### Two Steps to Integration

**Step 1: Focus on yourself**
Commit to your own growth across all three dimensions:
- Maintain a true Operating Manual
- Create your own Worthy Goals
- Be more coach-like in your interactions

**Step 2: Develop a system for your team**

*Audit relationships:*
- Evaluate the 3 Cs (Connection, Competence, Consistency) for each teammate
- Ask: How might the worthy goal not be working? Is coaching sufficient? Does the operating manual need revisiting?

*Match medium to component:*
- Operating manual: Create on your own, then meet live to share and diagnose
- Worthy goals: Track after a structured discussion
- Coaching: Integrate into weekly 1:1s

*Weekly 1:1 structure:*
- Open with "What's on your mind?"
- Use coaching questions throughout
- Close with "What was most useful or valuable here for you?"
- Prepare with asynchronous update documents (priorities, progress, problems) submitted 12 hours before the meeting. The synchronous time is reserved for problem-solving, coaching, and relationship building.

### Recommended Cadence

| Component | Frequency | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Operating Manual review | Every 6 months | 1 hour (varies from standard meetings) |
| Worthy Goal review | Every quarter | Discussion on progress and refinement |
| Coaching (1:1s) | Weekly | 30 minutes or less |

### The 30-Day Action Plan

A structured rollout for one teammate:
1. Schedule two meetings roughly 2 weeks apart
2. Communicate the intention casually and approachably
3. Meeting 1: Explain the operating manual concept, share the blank template
4. Between meetings: both parties complete their operating manuals
5. Share completed manuals before Meeting 2
6. Meeting 2: Share and discuss operating manuals with vulnerability and openness
7. Introduce worthy goals after operating manuals are established
8. Schedule recurring 1:1 check-ins with coaching questions

### Lessons from Olympic Coaches

Great Olympic coaches embody the Complete Manager attributes:
1. **Forge tight, trusting relationships** -- best coaches provide social and emotional support, not just technical guidance. They over-index on emotional intelligence.
2. **Reframe Worthy Goals from winning to self-improvement** -- prioritize improvement over outcomes. (When Phelps won a medal, "he was upset with his performance because he'd set an individual standard.")
3. **Give autonomy rather than top-down direction** -- "The athlete makes the decision, the coach helps see what is available." Coach-like questions: What are you going to do? Where are you going to train? With whom?

The pursuit of excellence requires compassionate support. All three dimensions -- trust, goals, coaching -- work together.

## Part 7: The 3-A Framework for Inclusive Leadership

From the Inclusive Leadership course with Mita Mallick.

**Core principle:** Inclusion starts at home, with family and community, then extends to the workplace. It is a journey, not a deliverable, and cannot be implemented via checklist.

### Access: Giving People a Seat at the Table

Providing access to where work gets done and decisions are made: meetings, brainstorms, offsites, executive conversations.

**Practical actions:**
- **Audit your calendar.** Check who is invited to your meetings and why. Notice who is there and who is not.
- **Reflect on intent vs. impact.** Three questions to ask:
  1. What is my intent here? What do I hope to achieve?
  2. How will the person feel? Will the intent match the impact?
  3. How can I ensure my positive intent lands with positive impact?
- **Use meeting invites as inclusion tools.** Three roles to assign:
  - **The Contributor:** If someone is doing the work being discussed, they should be included.
  - **The Observer:** Invite someone to observe and learn. Let them know their role. Pass note-taking around. Have them send insights post-meeting.
  - **The Host:** Invite a team member to host a meeting. An opportunity to recognize their work, share learnings, and help drive decisions with your support.

### Amplify: Creating Spaces for All Voices to Be Heard

**Practical actions:**
- **Allow space for all voices.** Invite people to contribute (never force). Send materials ahead so people can prepare. Give the option to provide thoughts in writing.
- **Give individuals credit for their work.** Allow them to present their own work. If they are not present, still credit them. Intervene when others take credit.
- **Stop "he-peating."** When someone's idea is ignored then repeated by another person to applause, redirect credit. Example scripts:
  - "Thanks for elevating [Name]'s suggestion, which she brought up earlier. [Name], let's circle back to you."
  - "Appreciate you reminding us of [Name]'s idea. [Name], can you tell us more about implementation?"
- **Actively facilitate.** Guide discussions, coach tough conversations, be the referee when needed. Do not allow individuals to talk over each other or monopolize airtime. Interject to take back control when necessary.

### Advocate: Actively Helping Others Understand a Person's Impact

The critical distinction between mentors and sponsors:

**Mentors:**
- Within or outside your organization
- Appropriate for new hires and transitions
- Help with systems, leadership styles, time prioritization
- Not in a position of power to advance careers

**Sponsors:**
- Within your organization, typically 2+ levels above
- Understand key power players and organizational politics
- Have budget, oversight, and influence over pay, promotion, and talent assignment
- See the value in what someone brings and their potential impact
- "Say my name when I am not in the room"

**Practical actions:**
- Ask how you can help
- Ensure equitable distribution of office work
- Identify someone you could sponsor
- Consider who you are fighting for behind closed doors
- Move from over-mentoring to active sponsorship

### Key Inclusive Leadership Principles
- Expanding social circles is the only real way to shatter stereotypes
- Even silence can be interpreted as inaction and misunderstood as negative intent
- There is a cost to being an inclusive leader (it takes effort and sometimes courage)
- It is never too late to start
- Start with one thing you will do differently on Monday

## Part 8: Leading Through Change -- The Three-Phase Framework

From the Leading Through Change workshop with Peter Wang.

Change is ultimately about people. The best leaders keep the "Survive" channel alert but not overwhelmed, while activating the "Thrive" channel in themselves and others.

| Survive Mode | Thrive Mode |
|---|---|
| Threat-seeking radar | Opportunity-seeking radar |
| Fast problem solving | Innovation and collaboration |
| Laser-focused | Broad perspective |
| Energy spikes | Energy increases |
| Anxiety, stress, anger | Curiosity, passion, excitement |

### Phase 1: Conviction -- Know When Change Is Necessary

Conviction is the spark for change. Before the organization buys in, the leader must recognize and assess opportunities and threats.

**"Listen to the Music" -- The 5 Forces of Change:**
1. **Consumers:** Will demand change?
2. **Suppliers:** Will cost of goods, capital, or talent change?
3. **Competitors:** Will existing competitors compete differently? Will new entrants emerge?
4. **Technology:** Will a technological development change how business is done?
5. **Complementors:** Will the price or landscape for complementary products change?

**Assessing Each Force -- Three Dimensions:**
- **Probability:** How likely is this change? (Most important factor. Low probability = no change, but begin contingency planning.)
- **Magnitude:** How impactful? (If probability is high and magnitude is industry-wide, capture first-mover advantage.)
- **Duration:** For how long? (Avoid large-scale changes if duration is short.)

**Building conviction requires good judgment** -- getting the destination right. No magic formula for the "right" combination of probability, magnitude, and duration, but these dimensions structure the analysis.

Conviction applies to both top-down change (leadership initiating) and bottom-up change (smaller teams working up to leadership).

**What if leadership has conviction but you do not?** Have an honest, objective conversation. Do not fake conviction.

### Phase 2: Commitment -- Craft a Compelling Change Narrative

Commitment is about effectively communicating change and gaining feedback and buy-in. Build a change narrative with two requirements:
- The **content** must be intellectually rigorous (the substance)
- The **framing** must be emotionally intelligent (the words, the tone)

**The Change Narrative Structure:**

**Why:**
- What is changing in the world, and why does it matter to us?
- Why is now the right time for us to change too?
- Include probability, duration, and magnitude to explain urgency
- Set the tone: frame as threat or opportunity (this determines whether you activate Survive or Thrive in your audience)

**What:**
- What type of change? (Initiating/accelerating, Pivot, Decelerate/eliminate)
- What is the scope of change?
- What are the mandates and time frame?
- What still needs figuring out and by when?

**Who:**
- **First degree:** Those directly impacted
- **Second degree:** Support and collaborators
- **Culture carriers:** People with no formal authority but a good pulse on the organization. Identify them to vet your change narrative before broader rollout.

### Phase 3: Closure -- See Change Through

Do not underestimate how fragile new changes are. Three pillars of closure:

**1. Communicate to Build Trust and Confidence**
- Uncertainty is one of the highest stressors
- Celebrate progress to fuel the Thrive channel
- Make progress visible: all-hands (teams present their own successes), real-time in Slack/Teams
- Be specific and tie back to the why
- Do not bury challenges. Trust goes up when you acknowledge them.
- Do not declare victory too soon and stop short of the finish line

**2. Nurture Cross-Silo Collaboration**
- Take time to institutionalize changes across the organization and processes
- Minimize survive-induced silo mentality
- Proactively suggest the right people to get involved when you notice gaps
- Define, track, and share team goals
- Establish new team rituals

**3. Culture of Care and Continuity**
- Change is a dynamic process requiring a continuous and honest feedback loop
- Methods for maintaining the loop:
  - Office hours (open to all)
  - Pulse surveys (team/org-wide)
  - Staff meetings (direct reports)
  - Direct report 1:1s
  - Skip-level 1:1s
  - Peer 1:1s
  - Strategic 1:1s
- Uncover blind spots through multi-level listening

### The 10/10/10 Framework

Help your team think about change in both the long and short term:
- After this change, what will this organization look like in 10 days?
- In 10 months?
- In 10 years?

### Top Takeaways for Leading Through Change
1. Change does not need to be reactive -- listen to the music and build conviction ahead of the shift
2. Frame your narrative by articulating what you are hoping to achieve, and the necessary change
3. Do not fake conviction -- if you or your team are not convinced, have an honest, objective conversation with leadership
4. Identify the culture carriers in your organization to vet your change narrative
5. When implementing change, serve as the bridge between the old way of business and the new

## Part 9: Integrated Leadership Toolkit -- Quick Reference

### The Prizes and Punishments Exercise

When considering whether to change or maintain the status quo, map the prizes and punishments of each path:

**Prizes of the status quo:** Less risk, easier time, no commitment required, no chance of being wrong

**Punishments of the status quo:** Company may fail, people miss the chance to do something great, sense of dissatisfaction at "what might have been," unlikely to advance, sense of mediocrity

Use this exercise to build conviction for change with your team.

### Decision-Making Model

A four-level model for matching decision style to context:
1. **Consensus** -- everyone agrees (use for high-stakes, time-available decisions)
2. **Consultative** -- gather input, leader decides (most common)
3. **Delegate** -- assign decision authority to others
4. **Directive** -- leader decides immediately (use for urgent, time-sensitive situations)

### Relationship Audit Rubric

For each teammate, evaluate across the three Complete Manager dimensions:
- **Trust:** Rate Connection, Competence, Consistency
- **Goals:** Is there a worthy goal? Is it thrilling, important, daunting?
- **Coaching:** How often are you being coach-like? Are you asking or telling?

Identify one teammate where the relationship needs the most work and start there.

## Part 10: Putting It All Together

The three courses form a coherent leadership development system:

1. **Start with trust** (Complete Manager Module 1). Build the Three Cs. Create and share operating manuals. Without trust, nothing else works.

2. **Set worthy goals** (Complete Manager Module 2). Use the Thrilling-Important-Daunting framework and the four-step drafting process. Connect individual goals to company objectives.

3. **Coach, do not tell** (Complete Manager Module 3). Tame the advice monster. Use the five coaching questions. Build a coaching habit through practice.

4. **Operationalize** (Complete Manager Module 4). Create a system with cadence: 6-month operating manual reviews, quarterly goal reviews, weekly coaching 1:1s. Focus on yourself first, then scale to your team.

5. **Lead inclusively** (Inclusive Leadership). Apply the 3-A Framework in every interaction: provide Access, Amplify voices, Advocate for others. Move from mentoring to sponsoring. Audit your calendar, your meetings, and your social circles.

6. **Navigate change** (Leading Through Change). When the environment shifts, move through Conviction (assess with the 5 Forces), Commitment (build a compelling narrative addressing Why, What, Who), and Closure (communicate, collaborate, care). Use the 10/10/10 framework for perspective.

These frameworks are mutually reinforcing. The trust you build through operating manuals creates the psychological safety needed for daunting worthy goals. The coaching questions sustain both trust and goal progress. Inclusive leadership practices ensure you are building trust and setting goals with your entire team, not just those who look or think like you. And when change arrives, a team with strong trust, clear goals, and a coaching culture adapts faster and with less distress.

The ultimate aim: be the manager who unlocks greatness in others while continuing to grow yourself.
