## Case Study: Aerie vs. Victoria's Secret -- Operational Laddering

Source: PDF case study, "Aerie: Laddering to Leverage Your Competitor's Weakness"

### The Business Context

Over six years, Aerie registered double-digit revenue growth every quarter. The firm grew from less than 2% to 7% of the US lingerie market. In the same period, Victoria's Secret's market share dropped from 79% to 65% (2016-2019). In January 2019, Google search interest for Aerie surpassed search interest for Victoria's Secret.

### Operational Strategy: Laddering Across the Business

Aerie's success is a case study in applying a strategic concept (laddering -- highlighting a brand strength that exposes a competitor's weakness) across all operational dimensions of a business, not just marketing.

#### 1. Operations: Product Strategy (Sizing and Inclusivity)

**The move:** More than 97% of Aerie products are available in sizes ranging from XXS to XXL.

**The competitive contrast:** As of 2020, only 4% of Victoria's Secret products were available in XXS and just 10% in XXL.

**Operational implications:**
- **Supply chain complexity:** Supporting a full size range across 97% of products requires more SKUs, more complex inventory management, and more nuanced demand forecasting
- **Addressable market expansion:** By taking an inclusive approach to sizing, Aerie widened its addressable market significantly
- **Competitive de-positioning:** Simultaneously positioned Victoria's Secret as exclusive and out of touch -- without ever mentioning them directly

**Key operational lesson:** Product operations decisions (what sizes to manufacture, how to manage the resulting SKU complexity) can be a strategic weapon, not just a logistics challenge.

#### 2. Operations: Marketing and Brand Operations

**The move:** In 2014, Aerie announced it would no longer photoshop images of models. In 2016, the firm began featuring "real" women of all body types through its "AerieReal" spokesperson program.

**The competitive contrast:** Victoria's Secret ads were dominated by sexualized images of airbrushed models.

**Operational implications:**
- **Content production:** Requires different photography processes, model selection criteria, and content approval workflows
- **Trend alignment:** Since 2014, searches for "body positivity" increased more than 300%
- **Cost structure:** Potentially lower content production costs (less post-production) while achieving higher consumer resonance

**Key operational lesson:** Marketing operations (how you produce content, what your creative process looks like) can reinforce strategic positioning if aligned with the brand.

#### 3. Operations: Events and Experience Design

**The move:** In 2020, Aerie launched the AerieReal Summit. Tickets cost $25, with sales donated to the National Eating Disorders Association.

**The competitive contrast:** For decades, tickets for Victoria's Secret's annual fashion show cost more than $18,000, with the front row reserved for celebrities.

**Operational implications:**
- **Event economics:** Low ticket price with charitable donation creates goodwill and brand alignment at minimal cost
- **Accessibility as strategy:** Democratized access vs. exclusionary gatekeeping
- **Outcome:** Victoria's Secret canceled its fashion show in 2019, citing declining viewership

**Key operational lesson:** Event operations and experience design should reinforce the same strategic message as product and marketing operations. Consistency across all operational touchpoints is what makes laddering effective.

### Takeaways for Operational Strategy

For firms looking to apply operational laddering:

1. **Leverage your competitor's weakness as your strength.** Highlighting this strength reveals the competitor's weakness without ever addressing them directly.

2. **Extend your strategy to all parts of the business:** product operations, marketing operations, and event/experience operations. Consistency is what makes it powerful.

3. **Operational decisions are strategic decisions.** Sizing, content production, event pricing -- these are not just logistics. They are expressions of brand strategy that compound over time.

## Framework: Operational Excellence as Competitive Advantage

Derived from the patterns in the Aerie case and the operational thinking.

### The Operations-Strategy Connection

Most companies treat operations as execution of strategy. The Aerie case demonstrates that operations IS strategy -- every operational decision either reinforces or undermines strategic positioning.

### Five Operational Levers for Competitive Advantage

**1. Product Operations**
- What do you make and for whom?
- How do you manage SKU complexity?
- How do you balance standardization with customization?
- Aerie example: inclusive sizing as an operational commitment that doubles as strategic positioning

**2. Supply Chain Operations**
- How do you source, manufacture, and distribute?
- What trade-offs do you make between cost, speed, and flexibility?
- Can supply chain decisions be a moat? (Amazon's logistics, Zara's fast fashion supply chain)

**3. Marketing Operations**
- How do you produce content?
- What processes govern creative decisions?
- How do you measure marketing effectiveness?
- Aerie example: eliminating photoshop as both a process change and a strategic statement

**4. Customer Experience Operations**
- How do you design and deliver customer interactions?
- What processes ensure consistency across touchpoints?
- How do you measure experience quality?
- Aerie example: events designed for accessibility and mission alignment

**5. Measurement and Feedback Operations**
- How do you track what is working?
- How do you connect operational metrics to strategic outcomes?
- How quickly can you adapt based on data?

### Operational Consistency Audit

For each operational lever, ask:
1. Does this operational decision reinforce or undermine our strategic positioning?
2. Would a customer recognize this as consistent with our brand?
3. Does this create a structural advantage that competitors cannot easily copy?
4. Does this operational choice highlight a competitor weakness?

## Strategic Predictions and Trend Analysis (Galloway)

The Galloway Predictions course (2022-2023) consisted of a single video recording analyzing market trends and making forward-looking predictions about technology, business, and the economy. No text notes or transcripts were available in the course materials.

### General Framework for Strategic Prediction Analysis

While the specific predictions content was multimedia-only, Galloway's prediction methodology (used across courses) follows a consistent analytical pattern:

**1. Identify Secular Trends**
- What structural shifts are underway (demographic, technological, regulatory)?
- Which trends have been accelerated by recent events?
- Which industries are most exposed?

**2. Apply the "Who Benefits?" Test**
- For any disruption: Who gains market share? Who loses?
- Follow the capital flows: Where is investment moving?
- Follow the talent flows: Where do the best people want to work?

**3. Evaluate Through the Three Hurdles**
- Is the trend differentiated (truly new, not just noise)?
- Is it relevant (does a large enough market care)?
- Is it sustainable (can it endure competitive response)?

**4. Look for Operational Implications**
- How should companies adjust operations to ride the trend?
- What operational capabilities become more or less valuable?
- What organizational changes does the trend demand?

## Quick-Reference: Operations Strategy Checklist

- [ ] Map all operational touchpoints that customers experience
- [ ] Evaluate whether each operational decision reinforces or undermines strategic positioning
- [ ] Identify competitor operational weaknesses that you can ladder against
- [ ] Ensure consistency across product, marketing, and experience operations
- [ ] Assess whether operational choices create sustainable competitive advantages (moats)
- [ ] Monitor secular trends and adjust operations accordingly
- [ ] Treat operational decisions as strategic decisions, not just logistics

## Key Takeaways

1. **Operations IS strategy.** Every operational decision -- from sizing to content production to event pricing -- either reinforces or undermines your competitive position.

2. **Consistency across all operational touchpoints compounds advantage.** Aerie's laddering worked because it was not just in marketing -- it was in product, marketing, and events simultaneously.

3. **Operational complexity can be a moat.** Supporting a full size range across 97% of products is operationally demanding. That demand is what makes it hard to copy.

4. **Align operational decisions with cultural trends.** Aerie's inclusive operations aligned with rising body positivity sentiment, turning an operational investment into a cultural movement.

5. **Never address competitors directly.** The power of operational laddering is that highlighting your operational strengths implicitly reveals the competitor's operational weaknesses.
