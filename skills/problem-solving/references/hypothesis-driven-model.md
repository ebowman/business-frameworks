# The Hypothesis-Driven Problem-Solving Model

> Based on courses taught by Jenny Tang (Jennie Tung), Partner at Bain & Company with 20+ years of strategic consulting experience. Synthesized from Problem Solving Sprint, Problem Solving Sprint 2, Problem Solving Workshop, and Problem Solving Workshop - On Demand.

---

## Overview

The Hypothesis-Driven Model (also called the "Pyramid Structure" or "Answer First" approach) is the core problem-solving framework used by top strategy consultants at firms like Bain & Company. It provides a structured, efficient method to tackle complex business problems by organizing thinking into two clear phases: **Context** and **Exploration**.

The framework is built on a fundamental insight: rather than drowning in data and hoping a solution emerges, you start with an educated guess (hypothesis) and systematically prove or disprove it. Think of it like a scientific experiment applied to business decisions.

---

## The Four Core Problem-Solving Skills

Before diving into the framework, understand that all problem solving rests on four foundational skills. The Hypothesis-Driven Model forces you to practice all four:

### 1. Framing the Problem

Understanding the context and figuring out the right question to ask. As Einstein reportedly said: "If I had an hour to solve a problem, I would spend 55 minutes thinking about the problem and five minutes working on the solution." The way you frame your problem ultimately determines your solution.

### 2. Deconstructing the Problem into Parts

Complicated business problems are typically multi-layered. Having the discipline to deconstruct a problem into smaller pieces helps you understand all the forces at play. This is where issue trees and MECE decomposition come in (covered below).

### 3. Prioritizing the Parts

Not all components of a problem are equal. Choosing where to focus your energy allows you to work efficiently. The 80/20 principle is the primary tool here.

### 4. Deciding on a Course of Action

Analysis without action is incomplete. The goal is to get to high-quality solutions quickly. Organizations that act fast typically outperform those that do not.

**Where these skills apply:** The same four skills work at every level -- from executive strategy questions ("Should Netflix start including ads?") to operational decisions ("How can Whole Foods increase deli profit by 0.5%?") to personal choices ("Should I buy an SUV or an electric car?").

## The Hypothesis-Driven Model: Two Phases

```
PHASE 1: CONTEXT                    PHASE 2: EXPLORATION
+--------------------------+        +---------------------------+
| Situation                |        | Hypothesis                |
| What is happening now?   |        | Your best initial answer  |
+--------------------------+        +---------------------------+
| Challenge                |        | Reasons (2-4)             |
| What obstacle exists?    |  --->  | Why you believe it's true |
+--------------------------+        +---------------------------+
| Critical Question        |        | Data & Evidence           |
| What must we answer?     |        | Proof for each reason     |
+--------------------------+        +---------------------------+
```

## Phase 1: Context

**Purpose:** Define the problem before you solve it. Since we are hardwired to seek solutions, it is tempting to rush into exploration mode. Doing so risks solving the wrong problem and wasting enormous time and energy.

### The Three Components of Context

**1. Situation** -- What are you currently working on? What is the current state of affairs?
- This is objective and factual
- Describes the landscape and relevant background

**2. Challenge (also called "Complication")** -- What obstacles stand in your way? What makes this hard?
- Also relatively objective
- Identifies tension, change, or conflict in the situation

**3. Critical Question** -- Based on the situation and challenge, what is the key question you are trying to solve?
- This is subjective and has many potential answers
- The way you phrase your question determines the scope of your answer
- Getting this right is the single most important step in the entire process

### How to Validate Your Critical Question

When you think you have the right critical question, reflect on three checks:

1. **What assumptions am I making** by asking the question this way?
2. **Am I solving a macro or a micro issue?** (Are you at the right altitude?)
3. **Who will benefit** from me solving this problem?

### The Danger of the Wrong Question: Real Examples

**Healthcare Clinic (Real Bain case):** A chain of healthcare clinics in Asia had declining customer satisfaction. Surveys showed the waiting room experience was the main driver. The CEO asked the head of patient care to fix it. She asked: "How can we optimize our waiting room experience?" She spent weeks visiting five-star hotels and airline lounges to design the Ritz-Carlton of waiting rooms. The CEO was furious. The right question was: "How can we reduce or eliminate the wait altogether?" Weeks of rework resulted from the wrong question.

**Call Center Example (Workshop anecdote):** A team asked "How do we optimize each agent's workflow?" After investigation, they discovered 50% of calls were about card activation -- and the root cause was that the phone number on the physical card had a larger font than on the website, so customers took the easy route and called. The better question was: "What are ways to prevent unnecessary calls to the customer care center?"

**Key principle:** There is no magic formula for finding the right question. It is a team sport. Collaborate with colleagues outside your immediate working group. Different perspectives pressure-test your assumptions.

### Formulating the Critical Question: Best Practices

- **Slow down to speed up.** Time invested in framing the right question saves far more time by minimizing rework downstream.
- **Get others involved.** Brainstorm how to properly frame the question with your team to get broader perspectives and understand wider repercussions.
- **Check your assumptions.** There is no definitive way to create the right question, but keeping assumptions in check helps you avoid the wrong path.
- **Resist peer pressure to rush.** When working in teams, at least one member will be eager to jump to hypothesis development. Remind them the problem deserves a well-thought-out critical question.

## Phase 2: Exploration

**Purpose:** Create an initial hypothesis, determine the key reasons behind your thinking, then find the data to prove or disprove the hypothesis. This process unbundles big, complex problems so you can understand and attack them quickly.

### Step 1: Formulate a Hypothesis

A hypothesis is your best educated guess at an answer to the critical question. Practical steps to form one:

1. **Reflect on previous work experiences.** Ask yourself what worked and what did not in similar situations.
2. **Collaborate with colleagues and industry experts.** Draw from collective experience and reach out to your networks for expert advice.
3. **Look for industry analogies.** What have similar organizations done? What can you learn from their wins and mistakes?
4. **Check the competition.** Have competitors made similar decisions? How did those turn out?
5. **Do not wait for the perfect answer.** Get it on paper and treat it as a living document. The definition of a hypothesis is that it can be proven wrong.

**Critical mindset:** Being hypothesis-first may feel uncomfortable. You are making an educated guess before you have all the data. That is the point. It focuses your research and keeps you efficient. Do not become obsessed with your first answer.

### Step 2: Determine Your Reasons (Using the 80/20 Principle)

Identify 2-4 reasons why you believe your hypothesis is correct. These are the things that, if proven true, would give you conviction.

**The problem:** You and your colleagues will likely generate dozens of potential reasons. This is overwhelming and inefficient to investigate them all.

**The solution -- the 80/20 Principle:** Pick the top 20% of reasons that drive 80% of the impact. Leave the rest on the back burner.

**How to identify your top 20%:**
- First, do a brain dump of every possible reason
- Then ask: Which reasons are the most important in terms of driving impact?
- Which ones will leadership or customers care about the most?
- Dwindle the list down to your top 2-4 reasons

**Why this structure matters:**
1. **Decompose the work** -- unbundle complexity by identifying the key components of your recommendation
2. **Prioritize the work** -- enable decisions around where to focus and where NOT to focus

### Step 3: Validate Reasons Through Data

For each reason, find objective evidence that your reasoning is sound. This ultimately proves or disproves your hypothesis.

**Types of data to gather:**

| Data Type | Description | Examples |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Secondary data (internal)** | Your own organizational material | Financial reports, operational metrics, loyalty data, strategy documents |
| **Secondary data (external)** | Published external research | Market and industry reports, competitor data, articles, benchmarks |
| **Primary research** | Data that does not yet exist; you must create it | Customer surveys, employee interviews, expert interviews, A/B tests |

**Rules for data gathering:**
- Do not cherry-pick data that supports your work. Find the most relevant information and follow it where it leads.
- If the data supports your reasons, you have a solid hypothesis. If it does not, your reasoning does not stand, and it is back to square one.
- Being hypothesis-first saves time: instead of researching every possible topic, you focus only on the data that proves or disproves your specific reasons.
- **Fight confirmation bias:** Turn your hypothesis on its head. Try testing the opposite. Can you easily find data that disproves your current thinking? If so, you may be letting opinion influence the answer.

**Why not start with data first?** While starting with data might reduce bias, leading with a hypothesis allows you to deconstruct and prioritize the problem quickly. You think 80/20, selecting only the most critical components and focusing on that data alone. This is far more efficient than open-ended research.

## Issue Trees and MECE Decomposition

Issue trees are the structural tool that supports the "deconstructing" skill. When you break a problem into its component reasons, you are building an issue tree.

### The MECE Principle

MECE stands for **Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive**:
- **Mutually Exclusive:** Each branch of your issue tree should not overlap with others. No double-counting.
- **Collectively Exhaustive:** Taken together, all branches should cover the entire problem space. Nothing important is left out.

### How Issue Trees Work in Practice

Your critical question sits at the top. Below it, your 2-4 key reasons branch out. Under each reason, specific data points and evidence form the supporting leaves.

```
Critical Question
    |
    +-- Reason 1
    |       +-- Data point A
    |       +-- Data point B
    |
    +-- Reason 2
    |       +-- Data point C
    |       +-- Data point D
    |
    +-- Reason 3
            +-- Data point E
            +-- Data point F
```

The 80/20 principle tells you which branches to investigate deeply and which to prune. The MECE principle ensures your branches are clean and comprehensive.

## Communicating Your Findings

Using the Hypothesis-Driven Model is not just a problem-solving framework -- it is an effective way to structure communication. Bain trains consultants to use this structure internally to run meetings efficiently and externally to communicate effectively to senior clients.

### The Pyramid Principle for Communication

The same pyramid structure applies to presentations, emails, and verbal updates:

1. **Establish Context** -- Situation, Challenge, and Critical Question (especially for senior stakeholders who are busy and need a refresher)
2. **Lead with the Recommendation** -- State your hypothesis and quickly outline the 2-3 main reasons (keep it high level; have detailed analysis ready in your back pocket)
3. **Explain Implications and Propose Next Steps** -- Show you can both think strategically and act on findings
4. **Invite Discussion and Q&A** -- Close with open dialogue

### Structuring Next Steps

When proposing next steps in your recommendation, consider:

- **Do you need more data** to further validate the hypothesis? (Especially critical with conflicting data)
- **What is a low-cost, low-risk way to test** your hypothesis in the real world?
- **What metrics will you track** and how often? Which KPIs best show whether your idea works?
- **What is your contingency plan or exit strategy?** What happens if you are not seeing success? When will you know?

### Communication Across Formats

**Presentation structure:**
- Slide 1: Context (Situation, Challenge, Critical Question)
- Slide 2: Recommendation (Hypothesis)
- Slide 3-5: Key Reasons with supporting data
- Slide 6: Implications and Next Steps

**Email structure:**
- Paragraph 1: Context
- Paragraph 2: Hypothesis / recommendation
- Paragraph 3: Key reasons (brief)
- Paragraph 4: Proposed next steps

**Verbal update (2-minute format):**
- 30 seconds: Context
- 30 seconds: Recommendation
- 45 seconds: Key reasons
- 15 seconds: Next steps / ask

### The BRIEF Framework (Alternative for Quick Updates)

- **B**ackground: Provide quick context on what prompted the update
- **R**easons: Explain why you are speaking now -- why should they pay attention?
- **I**nformation: Provide 2-3 key nuggets of information you want to share
- **E**nding: Decide on what note you want to leave the conversation
- **F**ollow-up: Anticipate questions and prepare answers in advance

### The "So What" Test

Every piece of data, every reason, every slide should pass the "so what" test. Do not just present facts -- always tie them to the implication. What does this data mean for the decision? What action does it suggest?

## Worked Example 1: Cost Cutting (Layoff Decision)

### Context

- **Situation:** You own a small online retail business specializing in outdoor gear. From 2020-2022, sales skyrocketed and you doubled staff. But times have changed -- last quarter revenue was down 25% year-over-year.
- **Challenge:** You are worried about short and long-term health of the business. You have a strategy to boost revenues, but may need to cut costs as well. Your largest expense is labor.
- **Critical Question:** Do I need to make layoffs as a short-term cost-cutting strategy?

### Exploration

**Hypothesis:** No, I can maintain short and long-term financial profitability without conducting layoffs. (Based on knowing the company is financially healthy with no significant upcoming expenses.)

**Reasons:**

1. **Productivity** -- Reducing headcount would hurt productivity and growth
2. **Timing/Runway** -- I can stay solvent for the next 18-24 months without cutting salaries
3. **Alternatives** -- We can cut other expenses to become more efficient

**Data:**

| Reason | Data Found | Conclusion |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| Productivity | Revenue per employee has steadily increased despite hiring spree; cutting staff slows growth trajectory by 10% next year | Supports hypothesis |
| Timing/Runway | Financial statements show enough cash to support current pay structure for 2 years; investment strategy review reveals ways to de-risk portfolio | Supports hypothesis |
| Alternatives | Cannot cut R&D without compromising growth strategy; CAN cut marketing costs by 10% without compromising return on ad spend | Supports hypothesis |

**Outcome:** Data backs up all three reasons. Hypothesis confirmed -- layoffs are not needed.

### Key Lessons from This Example

- The model offers a structured approach even for emotionally charged topics like layoffs
- Be objective with data: even when you want to cut R&D to support your hypothesis, accept when the evidence does not support it and search for alternatives
- When you are not sure which reasons matter most, brainstorm all potential reasons first, then filter down

## Worked Example 2: Product Expansion (Furniture Business)

### Context

- **Situation:** You run a small furniture business selling custom dining room furniture. Business is going well. You think expanding your product offering will help grow profits. You have already sold a few custom office furniture pieces as one-off requests.
- **Challenge:** You want to grow but are unsure of the right expansion path.
- **Critical Question:** Should I expand my business by launching custom office furniture?

### Exploration (First Pass)

**Hypothesis:** Yes, I should launch custom office furniture.

**Reasons:**

1. **Demand** -- My existing and/or new customers have a need for custom office furniture
2. **Internal Capabilities** -- I already have the necessary labor and resources
3. **Profitability** -- Launching will grow revenue faster than associated costs

**Data -- A Pivot Emerges:**

For demand, user interviews are conducted. Research reveals: 38% of furniture customers are interested in custom office furniture, compared to 34% for dining room furniture. However, 43% desire custom **bedroom** furniture -- a stronger case.

**Revised Hypothesis:** I should launch custom **bedroom** furniture instead.

**Data (Continued with revised hypothesis):**

| Reason | Data Found | Conclusion |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| Demand | 43% customer interest in custom bedroom furniture (higher than office at 38%) | Supports revised hypothesis |
| Internal Capabilities | Manual skills for tables/chairs are transferable; new materials needed but sourced | Supports hypothesis |
| Profitability | Sensitivity analysis shows profitability within one year, assuming existing dining room line holds steady | Supports hypothesis |

### Key Lessons from This Example

- Resist the urge to lead with data rather than a hypothesis. Being answer-first focuses your data hunt.
- Keep your eyes on the evidence and be willing to pivot. The data led to a better answer (bedroom instead of office furniture).
- You can revise your hypothesis as many times as needed. The framework is iterative.
- After completing the model, vet your reasoning with colleagues, especially product and marketing teams.

## Worked Example 3: Brand Differentiation (B2B Legal Tech)

### Context

- **Situation:** You are VP of Strategy at a mid-size B2B legal technology company.
- **Challenge:** Significant number of new market entrants; risk of getting lost in the sea of options.
- **Critical Question:** How can we differentiate ourselves given this crowded competitive landscape?

### Exploration (First Pass)

**Hypothesis:** I am significantly differentiated from competitors in the B2B legal technology space.

**Reasons:**

1. **Brand Recognition** -- Extremely strong brand recognition among legal teams
2. **Technology** -- Develop and push product updates faster than competitors due to agile technology advantage
3. **Customer Service** -- AI-driven, same-hour response time -- best in the industry

**Data and Results:**

| Reason | Research Method | Result |
|--------|----------------|--------|
| Brand Recognition | Brand recall survey vs. competitors | Less than 30% correctly identify brand. NOT a differentiator. |
| Technology | Benchmarking exercise vs. competitors | Middle of the pack on product updates, bugginess, ease of deployment. NOT a differentiator. |
| Customer Service | AI chatbot assessment + NPS comparison | Confirmed as a genuine point of differentiation. |

Only 1 of 3 reasons validated. Hypothesis must be revised.

### Revised Hypothesis and Second Exploration

**New Hypothesis:** My customer service is my key differentiation from competitors.

**New Reasons:**

1. Use of AI in customer service
2. The element of surprise and delight
3. Video tutorials for customers

**New Data:**

1. Speed and accuracy of AI-generated answers
2. Customer testimonials mentioning genuine delight
3. Video tutorial views relative to competitors

### Alternative Hypothesis (Third Iteration)

After discussing with colleagues, an entirely different hypothesis emerges: "I am differentiated because I solve a highly specific and relevant legal technology need for B2B customers." Begin the exploration phase again.

### Key Lessons from This Example

- The hypothesis-driven model is a launchpad for **iterative** problem solving, not a one-and-done exercise
- When you cannot validate your original hypothesis through data, apply your learnings to generate a new hypothesis and embark on a fresh round of reasoning
- Multiple iterations are normal and expected

## Running Example: Starbucks (End-to-End Walkthrough)

This example runs throughout the entire course as the primary teaching vehicle:

### Context

- **Situation:** During the pandemic, Starbucks rolled out innovations like curbside and walk-through pickup locations. These helped retain customers, sustain revenues, and keep customers safe.
- **Challenge:** COVID restrictions have rolled back. More people are comfortable picking up orders indoors. Starbucks is unsure whether COVID-based innovations are still necessary.
- **Critical Question:** Should Starbucks continue to invest in COVID-based innovations, such as walk-through pickup locations, even though restrictions are lifted?

### Exploration

**Hypothesis:** Yes, Starbucks should keep investing.

**Reasons (after applying 80/20):**

1. **Demand** -- There is demand to keep these features; continued expansion will attract new customers
2. **Financial Health** -- New locations will be profitable long-term
3. **Brand Image** -- Starbucks will still uphold its stellar brand reputation

**Data Plan:**

| Reason | Primary Research | Secondary Research |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Demand | Survey thousands of current/potential customers on interest in walk-through pickup post-COVID | In-store post-COVID consumer data: do pickup locations draw 20%+ more first-time customers? |
| Financial Health | Work with finance to build projected revenue/expense model; run A/B tests on revenue uplift | Existing store data comparing walk-through vs. non-walk-through revenue |
| Brand Image | Poll customers on brand perception if walk-through locations added | Competitor analysis: who made similar moves, how did it perform? |

**Result:** Data sources support all three reasons. Hypothesis has a solid foundation. Proceed to communication and next steps.

## When to Use the Hypothesis-Driven Model

The model works best under three conditions:

1. **You already have some familiarity with the situation.** For many people, the starting point will be a problem at work where you have relevant context and experience.
2. **You are under time pressure.** The approach keeps you focused and efficient rather than boiling the ocean.
3. **You are solving complex problems.** Simple problems do not need this depth of analysis.

## Practical Checklists

### Context Phase Checklist

- [ ] Situation is described factually and objectively
- [ ] Challenge identifies a specific tension, change, or obstacle
- [ ] Critical question is clearly articulated
- [ ] You have checked: What assumptions am I making?
- [ ] You have checked: Am I solving a macro or micro issue?
- [ ] You have checked: Who benefits from solving this?
- [ ] At least one colleague or team member has reviewed the critical question
- [ ] You have considered at least 2-3 alternative framings of the question before selecting one

### Exploration Phase Checklist

- [ ] Hypothesis is clearly stated as a direct answer to the critical question
- [ ] Hypothesis was informed by experience, colleagues, analogies, or competitive analysis
- [ ] 2-4 key reasons identified using the 80/20 principle
- [ ] Each reason is testable -- you can identify specific data that would prove or disprove it
- [ ] Reasons are MECE (mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive)
- [ ] For each reason, data sources have been identified (secondary internal, secondary external, primary)
- [ ] Data has been gathered objectively, not cherry-picked
- [ ] Confirmation bias has been checked by testing the opposite hypothesis
- [ ] If data disproves a reason, you have revised the hypothesis rather than ignoring the evidence

### Communication Checklist

- [ ] Context is established first (Situation, Challenge, Critical Question)
- [ ] Recommendation is stated upfront (lead with the answer)
- [ ] 2-3 key reasons are outlined at a high level
- [ ] Supporting data is ready in back pocket but not front-loaded
- [ ] Implications and next steps are proposed
- [ ] Format is appropriate (presentation vs. email vs. verbal) and concise
- [ ] Discussion/Q&A is invited

## Profile of a Great Problem Solver

A great problem solver, according to this framework:

1. **Understands the context** in order to ask the right question
2. **Validates or invalidates** their hypothesis with data and evidence
3. **Is 80/20** and does not try to boil the ocean
4. **Feels comfortable being proven wrong** and adjusts accordingly
5. **Turns insight into action quickly** -- analysis without action is incomplete
