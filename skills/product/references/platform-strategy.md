# Platform Strategy Reference

Source: Platform Strategy Sprint

## 1. What Is a Platform?

### Definition

A platform is a business model that creates value by facilitating connections between two or more groups of participants, enabling them to exchange value through a shared infrastructure with defined rules of engagement.

Platforms can be physical (bazaars, Visa/Mastercard) or digital (Apple, Amazon, Google, Uber, Airbnb).

### Four Characteristics of a Platform

1. **Connection** -- Brings together distinct groups (Airbnb: renters and hosts; Uber: drivers and riders)
2. **Multi-sided value exchange** -- At least two sides exchanging value. The value unit is the ride, room/night, gig, etc.
3. **Rules of engagement** -- Fees, complaint mechanisms, quality standards, trust mechanisms
4. **Underlying infrastructure** -- The technology, processes, and systems enabling the interactions

### Three Key Attributes: Platform vs. Product

| Attribute | Platform | Product |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Market structure** | Multi-sided market | One-sided market |
| **Value creation** | Non-linear (network effects) | Linear (make and sell) |
| **Value source** | External (leverages ecosystem participants' assets) | Internal (company owns and produces) |

### Network Effects

Network effects are what make platforms fundamentally different from products. The platform becomes more useful as more people join it.

**Direct network effects:** The platform is more attractive to one side as more users join that same side. Example: Sony PlayStation -- more attractive to consumers when there are more games.

**Indirect network effects:** The platform is more valuable to one side as the other side grows. Example: PlayStation is more valuable to developers as more gamers join. This is what distinguishes platform markets from one-sided product markets.

**Platforms typically need both direct and indirect network effects.** A platform with only indirect effects has a weaker competitive position.

### Technology vs. Transaction Platforms

| Type | Description | Examples | Who invests first |
|------|-------------|----------|-------------------|
| **Technology platforms** | End users on both sides invest in the platform's technology | Game consoles, smartphones, operating systems | Usually start with end-users (consumers) |
| **Transaction platforms** | Connect parties with under-utilized assets/skills to those who need them | Airbnb, Uber, Upwork, Etsy | Usually start with suppliers |

### Non-Tech Platforms

Platform thinking applies beyond technology:
- **Keurig** coffee pods: Opened the pod format to third-party manufacturers, disrupting Nespresso's closed ecosystem.
- **HMOs**: Connect patients, doctors, and insurers. More doctors attract more patients (and vice versa).

**Key questions for any business:**
1. Can one side be opened to third parties?
2. Can you take a different approach to connecting buyers and sellers?
3. Can a competitor come in and platform-ize your market?
4. Watch for early signs of disruption.

**Most markets can be converted into platform markets. A non-linear platform has massive advantages over linear product businesses. You will lose if you bring a knife to a gun fight.**

## 2. Why Platforms Matter

### Economic Power

- 4 of the world's 5 most valuable companies are platforms.
- Higher profit margins and lower marketing costs than traditional product firms.
- Revenue and market valuation accelerate more quickly.
- Google, Microsoft, Apple, Amazon employ 1.5M people collectively.

### Seven Reasons Platforms Matter

1. **Enormous economic value** -- Platforms dominate market capitalizations globally.
2. **Revenue efficiency** -- Lower marketing spend-to-revenue ratio due to network-driven growth.
3. **Monetize latent supply** -- iOS facilitates half a trillion dollars of commerce. Amazon's third-party marketplace generates more than half its revenue.
4. **Reduce costs** -- Apple does not create apps. Uber does not buy cars. Asset-light model.
5. **Resilience** -- More resilient to demand changes. During the pandemic, Airbnb's revenue AND costs went down together (revenue down 18% vs. 60% for hotels). Airbnb went public in late 2020 as one of the largest IPOs in history.
6. **Reduce friction** -- Reduce transaction risk, improve matching, add verification and rating mechanisms.
7. **Harder to disrupt** -- Customers compare ecosystems, not products. Deeper moat. Nokia made better phones but lost because it did not create an ecosystem.

### Geographic Scale

- Technology platforms (digital, bits-based) can scale globally: Android, iOS, Windows.
- Transaction platforms may need local critical mass: DoorDash must win city by city (500 restaurants in 1 city beats 5 restaurants in 100 cities).

## 3. The Platform Evolution Framework

Five evolutionary stages every platform goes through:

```
1. Inception --> 2. Scaling --> 3. Monetization --> 4. Protection --> 5. Expansion
```

---

## 4. Stage 1: Inception

### Core Principle

Do not create a solution before you understand the problem. 9 out of 10 startups fail, and the top reason is solving problems that do not have a market need.

### Four Key Steps to Identify Pain Points

1. **Identify key pain points for ALL participants** -- Not just one side.
   - Example: Rover (pet care) -- Pet owners struggle to find trusted care. Pet sitters and walkers struggle to find clients.

2. **Assess market fragmentation** -- The more fragmented on both sides, the more difficult it is for people to find each other, and the greater the platform opportunity.

3. **Evaluate the trust deficit** -- Can participants trust each other? (Letting a stranger into your home, trusting a stranger with your pet)

4. **Determine willingness to pay** -- What is the best alternative? How much would people pay for a better solution?

### Identifying Two Market Sides

After identifying demand (pain points), determine who has the supply. What talents, skills, or assets can they contribute? The key: external value from ecosystem participants, not constrained by your own capital or hiring.

### Latent Value

Arises when there is under-utilized, under-monetized, or under-appreciated capacity. Two empty bedrooms. An idle car. Unused professional skills.

**Sizing the market:** Number of participants on each side multiplied by transaction value and frequency. Uber needs a bigger market than Airbnb because average ride ($25) is much less than average night ($106).

### Creating the Value Proposition

Must address and remove pain points for BOTH market sides. Understand value drivers:
- **Choice and variety**
- **Trust and transparency**
- **Convenience**
- **Control**

All value drivers should add up to being **10x better** than the current alternative. Uber is 10x better because of all these things combined.

### Designing Interactions

Three types of interaction in a platform market:
1. Buyer <--> Seller
2. Buyer <--> Platform
3. Seller <--> Platform

Each interaction involves flows of information, money, and trust, supported by infrastructure.

### Reconceptualizing an Existing Business as a Platform

Any product or service business can potentially be converted:
- **High-value capital goods used infrequently** -- Sell unused capacity (aircraft hours, special machinery).
- **Product to service conversion** -- Tide could sell "clean laundry" instead of detergent, creating a platform connecting consumers, drivers, and cleaning services.

### Pitfalls

1. Solution looking for a problem (insufficient research on viability).
2. Market too small -- missing the bigger opportunity (Grubhub limited to restaurants doing their own delivery; DoorDash brought their own drivers and captured the market).
3. Legal or regulatory issues (gig economy labor regulations, tax policies).

### Indicators of Success

1. Important, unsolved problem in the marketplace
2. Large addressable market on both sides
3. Value proposition that is 10x better than alternatives
4. Clearly defined external value creation
5. All interactions clearly designed
6. Legal/regulatory issues considered

## 5. Stage 2: Scaling

### Core Challenge

The chicken-and-egg problem: How do you attract one side without the other?

### Three Steps to Scale

#### Step 1: Focus on One Side First

Do not try to build both sides equally. Choose which side to activate first:
- **Transaction platforms** usually start with suppliers (Etsy, YouTube).
- **Technology platforms** usually start with end-users/consumers (Apple, Nintendo).

#### Step 2: Inject Liquidity

Incentivize one side to join through seeding or subsidizing:

**Seeding strategies:**
- **Pay to join** -- PayPal gave $10 for signing up and for referrals.
- **Use your own network** -- Crowdspring asked friends and family to join.
- **Wizard of Oz** -- DoorDash team manually ordered, picked up, and delivered food, creating the illusion of a network.

**Subsidizing strategies:**
- Subsidize the more price-sensitive side and the side more likely to ignite network effects.
- **Freemium** (Spotify), **below-cost hardware** (Xbox sells consoles at cost), or **free** (Facebook).

#### Step 3: Zig-Zag

Once one side has critical mass, pull in the other side. Then balance:
- If you have fewer drivers than needed, offer bonuses to drivers.
- Then grow riders with discounts.
- Continue balancing supply and demand.

Some platforms build both sides simultaneously, but this is extremely expensive (Airbnb, Uber -- billions in venture capital).

### Pitfalls

1. **Failing to achieve critical mass on one side** -- Design Inc: high-quality design jobs are not given to random designers.
2. **Over-subsidization** -- HomeJoy subsidized house cleaning to $20; when prices rose to $100, demand collapsed.
3. **Logistics that do not add up** -- Lux Valet: $5/hour parking with valets was operationally unsustainable.

### Indicators of Success

- Sufficient scale on both sides
- Users activated (converted to meaningful interactions)
- Direct and indirect network effects activated
- High percentage of transactions going through the platform
- Low acquisition costs, high interaction quality

## 6. Stage 3: Monetization

### Core Challenge

Transitioning from subsidized growth to sustainable revenue. Many platforms get stuck in an infinite scaling loop. Once you withdraw artificial liquidity, is the business stable?

### Five Revenue Models

#### 1. Transaction Fees
- Simplest model. Best when transactions happen irregularly with varying values.
- Square: 2.6% from merchant, nothing from customer.
- Apple App Store: 15% transaction fee + 15% of in-app sales.
- Does not hamper network effects (only charged at transaction).

**Choosing the paid side:**
- Subsidize the more price-sensitive side and the side you need more.
- Charge the side that needs to deliver quality and whose demand increases as the other side grows (typically sellers/developers).
- Asymmetry is good. Trying to make both sides "the same" conflicts with profit maximization.

#### 2. Subscription Fees
- Best when service is used frequently, predictably, and long-term.
- Netflix, Skillshare, Adobe (switched to subscription in 2013).
- No cross-subsidy; fees based on willingness to pay.
- Can layer on top of transaction fees (DoorDash DashPass, Uber Pass).

#### 3. Premium Access (Freemium)
- Basic version free; pay for premium features.
- Spotify: free tier supported by ads, premium tier removes them.
- Requires confidence that sufficient free users will upgrade.

#### 4. Advertising
- Fully subsidize one side; charge the other for access.
- Facebook, Google.
- Only works with critical mass (advertisers need scale).
- Risk of negative network effects (too many ads ruin experience).

#### 5. Complementary Products and Services
- Layered on top of core model.
- Salesforce charges for training. Apple has Apple Music.

### Pitfalls

1. **Overly complicated business model** -- Keep it simple and transparent.
2. **Unsustainably high customer acquisition cost (CAC)** -- Uber still injecting promotional spending years after launch.
3. **Negative network effects** -- Growth reduces value for others. Too many promoted tweets diminish the experience. Fine line between monetization and ruining the product.

### Indicators of Success

- Clearly defined revenue stream and monetization approach.
- CLV is at least 3x CAC.
- Business model validated -- people actually pay.

## 7. Stage 4: Protection

### Core Principle

Just because you are successful today does not mean you will be around tomorrow. The hunter can become the hunted quickly. Myspace dominated in 2007; Facebook overtook it in less than a year after opening to third-party developers.

### Six Defensive Strategies

#### 1. Increase Switching Costs
The money, effort, and emotional costs of leaving. Apple's walled garden strategy: giving up iMessage, proprietary cables, hassle of moving data.

**Tactics:**
- Expand your network (if all friends are on Facebook, you stay)
- Create proprietary investments (learning Salesforce)
- Hardware investments (AirPods lock you in)

#### 2. Increase Multihoming Costs
Multihoming: using more than one platform for similar services (Netflix + Disney+ + Hulu). Many drivers use both Uber and Lyft.

**Tactics:**
- Loyalty programs
- Exclusive content contracts (gaming console makers sign exclusive deals with game studios)

#### 3. Prevent Disintermediation
Stop both sides from interacting directly outside the platform.

**Tactics:**
- Restrict access during interactions
- Create enough value that participants prefer the platform (Airbnb's 6% fee is worth it for recourse if something goes wrong)
- Goal: loyal customers, not hostages

#### 4. Platform Innovation
Continuously add new features, capabilities, and ways to use the platform. Facebook steamrolled Myspace by constantly innovating. Companies that stop innovating become Nokia or Blackberry.

- Nokia missed the app ecosystem
- Blackberry missed the phone becoming a media device

#### 5. Avoid Envelopment
Envelopment: a competitor absorbs all functions and user base of an existing platform.

- Real Networks was enveloped by Windows Media Player
- TomTom was enveloped by Google Maps

**Prevention:** Requires capital to acquire others before being enveloped. Salesforce bought Tableau and Slack to avoid Microsoft envelopment.

#### 6. Adapt Rules of Engagement
Improve trust, transparency, and quality of matching. Think of your platform as a state that you govern.

**Tactics:**
- Certified users, SuperHost programs (Airbnb Luxe)
- Liability insurance, risk assessment
- Safety rules, tipping guidelines
- Quality standards that differentiate from multihoming competitors

### Pitfalls

- **Anti-trust** -- Being too successful at protection can trigger anti-trust investigation. Facebook owns four of the most-downloaded apps of the decade.

### Indicator of Success

Simple: Are your people leaving, or are they staying? Have you plugged all the leaks?

## 8. Stage 5: Expansion

### Core Principle

Once protected, drive growth by looking beyond the initial addressable market and pursuing adjacent opportunities.

### Five Growth Directions

| Direction | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Geographic** | Same product, new geography | TikTok expanding globally |
| **Product** | New product to existing customers | Apple Watch extending the ecosystem |
| **Customer** | Same product, new customer segment | Rover expanding from dogs to cats |
| **Capability** | New product for new customer, leveraging existing assets | Amazon building AWS from internal infrastructure (now >50% of profits) |
| **Share of Wallet** | Same customer, same product, increase spend | Amazon Prime (cross-selling, upselling, loyalty) |

### Prioritizing Growth Directions

Not all directions are equally attractive. Prioritize by highest return, lowest risk:

1. **Product-market fit** -- Which direction solves the most urgent and important customer problem? Go back to the inception framework. Google Glass failed with consumers but might have worked in B2B industrial settings.
2. **Size of the opportunity** -- Total market size, willingness to pay, transaction size. DoorDash focused on suburban families (large meals) while Uber Eats targeted smaller urban orders.
3. **Required investments** -- How much capital is needed? Uber's autonomous vehicle division (ATG) required too much investment and took too long, so it was sold.

### The Onion Model

Think of expansion as concentric layers around your core business:
- Inner layer: core business generating current revenue
- Outer layer: expansion opportunity
- Key question: What is the ratio of outer layer revenue to inner layer revenue? TikTok's 2018 expansion increased its addressable market by 5x.

### Pitfalls

1. **Lack of focus** -- Startups die of indigestion, not starvation. Uber expanded too aggressively (ATG sold, Uber Freight being sold). Pursue the path of least resistance.
2. **Missing capabilities** -- Uber Freight: truck fleets and people are completely different markets. No competitive advantage, starting from scratch.
3. **Lack of monetization in new markets** -- Each new region starts from scratch (new riders, new drivers). Uber gave up on China, Latin America, SE Asia -- sold to local competitors for minority stakes.

## 9. Platform Strategy Decision Framework

### Is Your Business Ready for Platform Thinking?

Ask these questions:
1. Is there a fragmented market with pain points on multiple sides?
2. Is there a trust deficit that a platform could solve?
3. Is there latent/under-utilized capacity?
4. Can you create a value proposition that is 10x better?
5. Can you design interactions that create value for all participants?
6. Is the addressable market large enough on both sides?

### Platform vs. Product Checklist

| Question | Platform Answer | Product Answer |
|----------|----------------|----------------|
| Where is value created? | Externally, by participants | Internally, by the company |
| How does the product improve? | More users = better product (network effects) | Better engineering/design = better product |
| How do you scale? | Activate both sides of the market | Manufacture and distribute more |
| What do you own? | Infrastructure and rules | Assets and inventory |
| What is your moat? | Network effects, switching costs, data | Brand, patents, scale |

### Key Lessons

1. Platforms generate enormous value through connections, low friction, and convenience.
2. The rules for building and competing in platform markets are fundamentally different from traditional strategy.
3. Network effects create winner-take-most dynamics, but require careful nurturing.
4. Every stage of the evolution framework has specific pitfalls and indicators of success.
5. If you do not understand platforms, someone will platform-ize your market and disrupt you.
6. Most markets can be converted to platform markets -- and non-linear value creation has massive advantages over linear business models.
