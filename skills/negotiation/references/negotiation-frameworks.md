# Negotiation Frameworks

Reference document synthesized from the "Negotiation" workshop (taught by Brian McDowell). Covers the 5P framework, planning canvas, cognitive biases, salary negotiation, and deal structuring.

---

## Core Philosophy

Negotiation is a game -- make it fun. Approach every negotiation with preparation, self-awareness about your biases, and a structured framework. Winning in negotiation does not mean the other side loses; the best outcomes create value for both parties.

---

## The 5P Negotiation Framework

The central organizing model for all negotiations. Think of negotiation as a game with five components:

### 1. Principles

**Definition of winning and losing.**

Before entering any negotiation, define what success and failure look like. This includes your goals, your interests (the "why" behind your goals), your walk-away point, and your BATNA.

**Key elements to define:**
- **Goal:** What specific outcome are you seeking?
- **Interests:** What underlying needs drive your goal? (compensation, growth, stability, recognition)
- **Walk-Away:** At what point do you leave the table?
- **BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement):** Your best option if no deal is reached

**The other side's principles:**
- What is their goal?
- What are their interests?
- What is their walk-away?
- What is their BATNA?

### 2. Parameters

**The rules of the game.**

Parameters define the boundaries and structure of the negotiation: what is negotiable, what is not, what precedents exist, and what constraints each side operates under.

- What issues are on the table?
- What are the known constraints (budget, timeline, authority)?
- What precedents or benchmarks exist?
- What information is public vs. private?

### 3. People

**Players, positions, playing style, how to win.**

Understanding the human element is critical. This includes:
- Who are the decision-makers?
- Who are the influencers?
- What is each person's negotiation style?
- What biases might each party bring?
- What is the relationship history?
- What is the power dynamic?

### 4. Process

**The timeline and plays in the game.**

How the negotiation will unfold:
- What is the sequence of events?
- When do offers and counteroffers happen?
- What is the timeline and deadline pressure?
- Are there multiple rounds?
- What communication channels will be used?

### 5. Plan

**The game plan.**

Your preparation document that synthesizes the other four Ps into a specific action plan:
- Opening position and rationale
- Concession strategy (what you are willing to give, in what order)
- Key arguments and evidence
- Fallback positions
- Walkaway triggers

## The Negotiation Planning Canvas

A structured worksheet for preparing any negotiation. Complete this before entering the negotiation.

### Your Side

| Element | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Goal** | What specific outcome do you want? |
| **Interests** | Why do you want it? What underlying needs does it serve? |
| **Walk-Away** | The minimum acceptable outcome -- below this, you leave |
| **BATNA** | Your best alternative if this negotiation fails |

### Their Side

| Element | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Goal** | What do they want to achieve? |
| **Interests** | Why do they want it? What drives them? |
| **Walk-Away** | What would make them leave the table? |
| **BATNA** | What are their alternatives? |

### Applied Example: Salary Negotiation

**Your Side:**
- **Goal:** Secure a job with salary at or above current compensation, with potential for significant equity upside (stock options/IPO)
- **Interests:** High compensation, stock options/IPO potential, job stability, a role that values experience and cultural contribution
- **Walk-Away:** Any offer that does not meet compensation requirements or does not promise significant growth potential
- **BATNA:** Stay in current role or choose between multiple offers if they arise

**Their Side:**
- **Goal:** Hire a highly skilled, culturally fitting candidate within budget
- **Interests:** Cost-effectiveness, candidate's skill and experience, cultural fit, long-term contribution
- **Walk-Away:** Candidate demands significantly higher compensation than budgeted, or does not align with company culture
- **BATNA:** Other candidates in the pipeline, or re-evaluating the role's necessity

## Interests vs. Positions

One of the most important distinctions in negotiation:

- **Position:** What someone says they want ("I want $150K salary")
- **Interest:** Why they want it ("I need financial security for my family" or "I want to feel valued")

**Why this matters:**
- Positions are often incompatible; interests frequently are not
- When you understand interests, you can find creative solutions that satisfy both parties
- Multiple positions can satisfy the same interest (salary, equity, signing bonus, flexible work all address "financial security")

**How to uncover interests:**
1. Ask "why" -- "Why is that important to you?"
2. Ask "why not" -- "Why wouldn't the alternative work?"
3. Listen for emotional language that signals underlying needs
4. Consider the other party's constraints and pressures

## Cognitive Biases in Negotiation

Self-awareness about biases is critical. Before every negotiation, identify your top biases and consider how they might affect your approach.

**Common negotiation biases:**

1. **Anchoring:** Over-relying on the first number or piece of information presented. The first offer sets the psychological frame for the entire negotiation.

2. **Confirmation bias:** Seeking information that confirms what you already believe while ignoring contradictory evidence.

3. **Loss aversion:** Feeling losses more strongly than equivalent gains. May cause you to accept a bad deal to avoid the "loss" of no deal.

4. **Overconfidence:** Overestimating your bargaining position or the strength of your BATNA.

5. **Status quo bias:** Preferring the current state even when change would be beneficial.

6. **Reactive devaluation:** Devaluing proposals simply because they come from the other side.

7. **Winner's curse:** If the other side accepts immediately, you offered too much.

**Mitigation strategy:**
- Before each negotiation, identify your top 1-2 biases
- Consider how those biases might specifically impact the negotiation at hand
- Assign a "bias check" role to a colleague or advisor

## Salary Negotiation Playbook

### Preparation Phase
1. Research market rates for the role (Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, industry surveys)
2. Complete the Negotiation Planning Canvas for both sides
3. Identify your BATNA (competing offers, current role)
4. Determine your walk-away number
5. List your non-salary interests (equity, flexibility, title, development budget)

### Execution Principles
1. **Let them make the first offer** when possible -- it reveals their range
2. **Anchor high but justifiably** -- use market data and your unique value
3. **Negotiate the total package,** not just salary: equity, bonus, signing bonus, vacation, remote work, professional development, title
4. **Express enthusiasm first** -- "I'm very excited about this role. I'd like to discuss the compensation to make sure it works for both of us."
5. **Use silence** -- after making your ask, stop talking
6. **Get it in writing** -- verbal offers are not binding

### When You Have Multiple Offers
- Your BATNA is strongest when genuine
- You do not need to reveal specifics, but can reference "competitive offers"
- Use the competing offer to create urgency without ultimatums

## Deal Structuring Principles

### Creating Value (Expanding the Pie)
- Identify differences in preferences, priorities, risk tolerance, and time horizons
- Trade items you value less for items you value more
- Look for mutually beneficial trades

### Claiming Value (Dividing the Pie)
- Know your reservation price (walk-away)
- Understand their reservation price
- The negotiation range is the space between these two points (ZOPA: Zone of Possible Agreement)

### The Negotiation Dance
1. **Open:** State your position and rationale
2. **Explore:** Ask questions, listen, identify interests
3. **Signal:** Indicate flexibility on some issues
4. **Package:** Make multi-issue proposals, not single-issue demands
5. **Close:** Summarize agreement, confirm understanding, document

## Quick-Reference Checklist: Preparing for Any Negotiation

- [ ] Define your goal, interests, walk-away, and BATNA
- [ ] Research and estimate the other side's goal, interests, walk-away, and BATNA
- [ ] Identify all negotiable issues (not just the obvious ones)
- [ ] Rank your priorities and theirs
- [ ] Identify your top cognitive biases and plan to mitigate them
- [ ] Prepare your opening position and rationale
- [ ] Plan your concession strategy (what to give, in what order)
- [ ] Anticipate their likely arguments and prepare responses
- [ ] Identify potential value-creating trades
- [ ] Define your walk-away trigger clearly before entering the room
- [ ] Practice your key talking points

## Key Takeaways

1. **Preparation is the differentiator.** Most people wing negotiations. Completing the planning canvas puts you ahead of 90% of counterparts.

2. **Focus on interests, not positions.** This is the single biggest unlock for finding creative, mutually beneficial solutions.

3. **Know your BATNA and theirs.** The party with the better alternative has more leverage. Strengthen your BATNA before the negotiation begins.

4. **Recognize your biases.** Self-awareness about anchoring, loss aversion, and overconfidence prevents costly mistakes.

5. **Negotiation is a learnable skill.** Treat every interaction -- from salary discussions to vendor contracts to internal resource allocation -- as practice.
