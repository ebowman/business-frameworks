## 1. Product Principles: The Three Levels of a Product

### Definition

Every successful product has three distinct levels, each essential to its success:

1. **Actual Product** -- The central, functional product. A car that drives, a phone that makes calls, the consulting service itself. It must function as intended.
2. **Augmented Product** -- Extra features or services that differentiate from competitors. Extended warranties, training sessions, superior customer service, AppleCare. Often used to distinguish in crowded markets (airlines differentiate through lounges, priority boarding, chauffeur services).
3. **Core Product** -- The psychological reason for the product's existence. The deepest level -- addresses a fundamental human need like status, security, belonging, or environmental consciousness.

### Why the Core Product Matters Most

A product that functions perfectly (great actual product) but fails to meet a core psychological need will fail. Examples of products with great actual products but no core product:
- **Segway** -- Designed to replace walking. The actual product worked, but nobody needed to replace walking. After 20 years, only 140,000 units sold before shutting down in 2020.
- **Google Glass** -- Technically impressive but did not address a consumer core need.

Conversely, products that nail the core product dominate:
- **Tesla** -- Beyond a great electric car (actual), it addresses status, environmental consciousness, innovation identity (core).
- **Ferrari** -- You might buy it for speed (actual), but most buy it for status (core).

### Actionable Process

1. **Before designing anything**, ask: what core need are we trying to meet? Without this, you cannot make product decisions about naming, packaging, pricing, features, or market positioning.
2. **Identify customer needs segment by segment** and prioritize by importance.
3. **Address high-priority needs with the core product**, conveying value through cues in actual and augmented products.
4. **Use the marketing toolkit** (price, placement, packaging, promotion) to contextualize the product for each segment.
5. **Continuously re-evaluate** to ensure core, actual, and augmented products remain clear and address real psychological needs.

## 2. The Psychology of Value

### Psychological Value

Psychological value is how valuable a product feels to someone subjectively. It differs by customer segment based on their needs. Products anchored in human needs carry meaning that drives purchasing.

### Maslow's Hierarchy as a Product Lens

Use Maslow's hierarchy to understand what drives people to buy:
- **Basic needs** (security, warmth, food) -- Poland Spring, Dasani (quench thirst)
- **Belonging/affiliation** -- Honest Company (shared eco-friendly values)
- **Esteem/status** -- Kanye West x APC T-shirt ($120 vs. $12 Old Navy)
- **Self-actualization** -- Voss Water (iconic glass bottle, aspirational lifestyle)

### Time as an Unmet Need

One of today's biggest unmet needs is time. Products that save time enable customers to become smarter versions of themselves. Uber, One Medical, Notion, and Hey.com all have a core product centered on giving back time.

### Process for Creating Psychological Value

1. Identify customer needs, segment by segment.
2. Prioritize needs by importance for each segment.
3. Address high-priority needs with the core product.
4. Convey value through cues in actual and augmented products (transparent pricing, 24/7 support, etc.).
5. Use price, placement, packaging, and promotion to contextualize.

**Key principle:** Think about customer needs first, not product features. You cannot be everything for everyone -- choose customer segments wisely.

## 3. Finding Your Niche (Target Market)

### Definition

Product-market fit is "being in a good market with a product that can satisfy that market" (Marc Andreessen). The niche is a set of consumers who share the same unmet need.

### Four Criteria for a Strong Niche

| Criterion | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Size** | Enough spending power to drive revenue and value | Depends on product price -- Jeff Bezos alone for a billion-dollar product, or millions for a small product |
| **Identifiable** | Can determine if a consumer fits the niche | Measurable characteristics, not vague traits |
| **Accessible** | Can reach them through available channels | If online, they must be online; if TV, they must watch TV |
| **Similar behavior** | Members respond similarly to pricing, distribution, and marketing | If some coupon and others do not, redefine the niche |

### Need-Based Segmentation Over Demographics

Traditional demographic segmentation (age, gender, location) is a blunt instrument. A 50-year-old in Brooklyn behaves differently than a 50-year-old elsewhere.

**Better approach:** Benefit-based segmentation -- identify what people are looking for from your product. If a whole group seeks the same benefit, that is a valid segment.

### Examples

- **Jitterbug** (2006): Identified older adults whose health, skepticism, or lack of know-how created a need for a simpler cell phone. 900,000 subscribers, $300M revenue, acquired by Best Buy for $800M.
- **Facebook** started as college-only. **Amazon** started as a bookstore. Both had clear niches before expanding.

### Practical Test

Ask: "Who is my product NOT for?" Excluding groups helps articulate who does fall within the niche.

## 4. Levels of Competition

### Four Levels of Substitutes

Products compete at multiple levels. Defining the right competitive set separates winners from losers.

| Level | Description | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| **Narrow** | Nearly indistinguishable products | Coke vs. Pepsi, Boeing vs. Airbus |
| **Form** | Similar products with different features/use cases | Allbirds vs. Timberland, Grand Theft Auto vs. Candy Crush |
| **Need** | Different actual products satisfying the same core need | Ferrari vs. luxury watch (both satisfy status need). Coffee vs. caffeine tablets |
| **Resource** | Competing for the same finite resource (money, time, attention, energy) | Amazon vs. Coca-Cola (competing for the same dollar). Netflix vs. sleep |

### Where to Focus

- **Commodity markets** (Coke/Pepsi) must address narrow substitutes.
- **Most successful companies** focus as much or more on need and resource competition. Netflix's biggest competitor is sleep, not Hulu.
- **B2B companies** must think about budget-level resource competition -- an IT budget covers hardware, software, hosting, email, and project management.

**Key insight:** Fox News does not compete with MSNBC (despite appearances). When Fox viewers leave, they go to National Review, Wall Street Journal, or Ben Shapiro -- need-level substitutes.

### Process

1. Identify all four levels of competition.
2. Decide which level is most important given your market.
3. Conduct competitive analysis at every strategic decision point.
4. Audit annually to catch new entrants and shifting landscapes.

## 5. Friction Audits

### Definition

A friction audit systematically identifies and removes stopping cues -- moments where consumers are confused, slow down, or disengage from your product.

### Three Phases

**Phase 1: Exploration**
- Observe customers engaging with the product in natural settings.
- Identify every touchpoint on a continuum from low complexity to high complexity.
- Look for stopping cues -- any moment that incentivizes disengagement.

**Phase 2: Intervention**
- Remove stopping cues one at a time.
- Ask at each point: Is there a way to require less energy from the customer? Can this be automated? Was this touchpoint necessary?

**Phase 3: Confirmation**
- Verify that removing stopping cues actually improved engagement.
- Measure the impact.

### Examples of Stopping Cue Removal

| Company | Stopping Cue Removed | Result |
|---------|---------------------|--------|
| Netflix | Episode endings (Post Play auto-plays next episode) | More episodes watched |
| Amazon | Payment friction (1-click ordering, saved cards, Subscribe & Save) | Increased purchases |
| Amazon Go | Taking out your wallet (automatic payment) | Removes pain of paying |
| IKEA | Store navigation confusion (unidirectional flow) | Customers see nearly every product |
| Casinos | Clocks and windows (removed sense of time passing) | Longer play sessions |

### Recurring Revenue (Rundles)

Subscription services remove the stopping cue of deciding whether to pay each month. Automating resubscription is an extremely powerful retention mechanism.

### Ethical Considerations

Always ask: "What is the worst that can happen when I remove this friction point?" Are you enlarging the pie (making lives better while gaining revenue) or creating predatory effects (robbing time and money)?

**Key principle:** Often it is smarter to spend money removing friction (sticks) than adding sweeteners (carrots).

## 6. Product Loops

### Definition

A product loop is a feature built into a product that brings people back over and over. The goal is building long-term relationships, not creating addicts.

### Four Strategies for Building Product Loops

#### 1. Embed Loops in the Actual Product
The product does not function properly without the loop-generating element.
- **Workflow products** (Calendly): Built into organizational workflow, used every time meetings are scheduled.
- **Ecosystem/razor-blade model** (Nespresso, PlayStation, Kindle): Initial purchase locks customers into recurring purchases. Nespresso sells 10B+ capsules/year ($6B+ revenue).

#### 2. Rewards (Augmented Product)
- **Extrinsic rewards**: Tangible monetary value. Airline miles programs (points, badges, leaderboards).
- **Intrinsic rewards**: Internal psychological rewards from effort. Freerice.com (donate rice by learning vocabulary), Duolingo (streak rewards, badges).

#### 3. Community
Social connection drives continual engagement.
- Instagram beat Hipstamatic by adding sharing (community) to photo filters.
- Lululemon stores include yoga spaces.
- Referral programs (Binance: 40% of friends' trade commissions).

#### 4. Content
Brand content reminds customers between purchases.
- John Deere's "The Furrow" magazine (120+ years old).
- Blue Apron menu emails between deliveries.
- Glossier started as the "Into the Gloss" blog before becoming a cosmetics company.

**Multiple strategies can overlap.** Peloton uses all four: ecosystem (bike + subscription), rewards (badges, leaderboards), community (referrals), and content (world-class instructors).

### Avoiding Dark Patterns

- **Liking vs. Wanting**: Users should both want and like your product. Wanting without liking = addiction.
- **Dog-food your products**: If you yourself use the product and it brings you joy, it will likely do the same for customers. Steve Jobs would not let his kids use the iPad (a violation). Nintendo's Miyamoto constantly played his own games.
- **Hippocratic Oath for products**: Above all else, do no harm.

## 7. Product Experimentation and De-Risking

### Why De-Risking Matters

The hard part is not how to build, but what to build. Successful companies still produce expensive failures (Juicero: $120M, Fire Phone: $170M, Zune: $280M). In all cases, they built full-fledged products before reducing the risks that killed those ideas.

**Better approach:** Fail quickly and cheaply. Like pharmaceutical companies screening thousands of molecules, or Pixar's Ed Catmull: "To be wrong as fast as you can is to sign up for aggressive, rapid failure."

### Three Questions of De-Risking

1. **What to test?** Identify the risks that can kill the venture. Break them into smaller, manageable pieces.
2. **How to test?** Design experiments to take risks off the table without spending too much.
3. **When to test?** Sequence experiments to achieve maximum risk reduction.

### Risk Decomposition

Decompose big risks into the smallest possible elemental risks across three buckets:

| Category | Key Questions |
|----------|--------------|
| **Demand risks** | Will people want it? How many? At what price? Through which channels? |
| **Supply risks** | Can we make it? Technology feasibility? Operations? Partnerships? |
| **Value risks** | Can we generate more revenue than costs? What market share? What pricing? |

**Process:**
1. Define a clear success objective ("increase revenues by 15%").
2. Decompose into demand, supply, and value risks.
3. Use the "5 Whys" technique -- go at least 5 layers deep to reach elemental risks.
4. Turn implicit assumptions into explicit risks. Expect 40-50 risks per venture.

**Superhuman example:** "Would busy professionals even want a new email?" Only 22% were "very disappointed" initially. Within months, 58%. Used to mitigate demand-side risk.

**Better Place cautionary tale:** Raised $200M, spent $800M over 6 years without adequately decomposing demand and value risks. Had correctly predicted EV market embrace but failed to test subscription pricing, driver interest, and fleet operator demand.

### Risk Mitigation Strategies

**Two types of information:**
- **Secondary data** -- Generated by others. Cheap, accessible, useful early on (Googling, industry research, competitor products).
- **Primary data** -- Behavioral data from your own experiments. More expensive but provides competitive advantage. Actions, not words.

**Experiment types (from cheapest to most expensive):**

| Technique | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Landing page** | Market the product without building it | Zapier built a landing page with high-fidelity mockups; validated customer demand |
| **Kickstarter/Pre-sale** | Test consumer interest before building | Oculus raised $2M; 200K+ projects funded on Kickstarter ($6B+) |
| **Wizard of Oz** | Human replicates product behavior behind the scenes | SRI computer mouse: person translated mouse movements to screen; DoorDash manually delivered orders |
| **Prototype** | Build only what is needed to test the key risk | Allbirds: 200 prototypes testing wool durability. iPet: iPhone case + SMS, no app. Mira AR: smartphones + 3D printer + fishbowls |
| **A/B test** | Compare variants with real users | Buffer: A/B/C pricing test before building the functional prototype |

**Prototype principles:**
- Test only ONE high-risk variable at a time.
- Build only what is critical; everything else stays manual.
- Start with the highest quantified risk -- the most make-or-break component.

### Sequencing Experiments

The order in which you run experiments matters. Good sequencing is the secret to de-risking.

**Sequencing process:**
1. Start with the de-risking board: all risks with degree of doubt, importance, total risk factor, selected experiment, and estimated cost.
2. **Estimate risk reduction** per experiment (how much will the degree of doubt decrease?).
3. **Calculate benefit-to-cost ratio** = (risk reduction) / (experiment cost).
4. **Rank experiments** by benefit-to-cost ratio in descending order. Run the highest ratio first.

**Allbirds example:**
- Wool material prototype: Risk reduction = 10 points, cost = $2,000, ratio = 5
- Kickstarter (DTC model test): Risk reduction = 4 points, cost = $4,000, ratio = 1
- Run the wool prototype first (5x better ratio).

**Key principles:**
- Assessments are subjective but give rough structure. Do not over-index on precision.
- The sequence is only the initial round. After each experiment, revisit the entire process -- risk factors, new risks, re-sequence.
- Like GPS routing: after every waypoint, you get new information about traffic and weather.

## 8. The Hooked Model (Product Engagement)

### Definition

The Hooked Model (Nir Eyal) is a design framework that connects a user's problem with your product with enough frequency to form a habit. The cycle: **Trigger --> Action --> Variable Reward --> Investment**.

### The Habit Zone

- A habit is an impulse to do a behavior with little or no conscious thought.
- Nearly half of daily actions are habits.
- The more frequently a behavior occurs (up to once per week), the more likely it becomes a habit.
- Products in the "habit zone" have sufficient **frequency x perceived utility**.
- For infrequently used products, bolt on frequent content or community (e.g., Williams-Sonoma cooking site, Nike Running Club).

### Step 1: Triggers

**External triggers** -- Calls to action (notifications, pings, emails). Training wheels for habits.

**Internal triggers** -- Uncomfortable emotional states (boredom, loneliness, fear, indecisiveness). The real engine of habits.

**Process:**
1. Create a customer persona (age, gender, goals, pain points).
2. Identify the internal trigger using 5 Whys (e.g., LinkedIn: check messages --> avoid missed opportunities --> looking for new job --> don't like current job --> feel excluded).
3. Establish context: What are they doing, where, when?
4. Design external triggers that immediately follow the internal trigger and appeal to its relief.
5. Visualize the association: the mental connection between trigger and product (e.g., "too tired to cook" = open delivery app).

**Prioritize triggers by frequency first, then magnitude.**

### Step 2: Actions

The user performs the intended behavior. Make it as easy as possible.

**Six hurdles (BJ Fogg's Ability Chain):**
1. **Time** -- The longer it takes, the less likely
2. **Money** -- The more it costs, the less likely
3. **Physical effort** -- More effort = less likely
4. **Mental effort** -- Cognitive load reduces likelihood
5. **Non-routine** -- Unfamiliar = less likely
6. **Social deviance** -- Not socially accepted = less likely

**Heuristics to lower hurdles:**
- Anchoring: "Was $100, now $49"
- Scarcity: "Only a few remaining"
- Framing: "Wanna Get Away?" (Southwest)
- Endowed progress: Progress bar starts at 80%

**Not all friction should be removed.** Sometimes increase friction in the user's interest (e.g., trading apps adding cognitive speedbumps).

### Step 3: Variable Rewards

Variability creates engagement and focus. Three types of reward:

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Tribe** | Acceptance and community; rewards from other people | Social media likes, comments |
| **Hunt** | Information, content, or monetary value | Ross Stores treasure hunts (90% off surprises); email (what news?) |
| **Self** | Personal gratification, competency, mastery | Candy Crush levels, Apple Health ring closure |

**Process for designing rewards:**
1. Identify the emotion to transition customers toward (bored --> entertained; anxious --> calm).
2. Determine appetite for variability (delivery apps reduce variability with progress maps; social feeds increase it).
3. Brainstorm 5-10 rewards pushing customers toward the target emotion.
4. Prioritize by biggest impact, lowest cost.

**Rewards of the tribe have the longest-lasting impact and can supply infinite variability.**

### Step 4: Investments

Small actions users take that improve the product with use and increase the likelihood of returning.

**Why investments work:** Reciprocity -- place the investment phase after the reward so the user feels compelled to give back. The IKEA Effect: if users invest in the product, they value it more.

**Two types:**

**Storing value:**
- Content (Dropbox), Skill acquisition (Adobe), Data (Mint), Reputation (Airbnb, eBay), Followers (Twitter), Credit (Starbucks rewards, frequent flyer miles)

**Loading the next trigger:**
- Sending a WhatsApp message creates an expected reply (external trigger) that pulls users back through the hook.

**Pitfalls:**
- Asking too much investment at once (ask for bits of information over successive passes).
- Taking too long to engage after loading the next trigger.

### Testing Hooks (Habit Testing)

**Metric:** % of habituated users (not DAU, which is inflated by ad spend spikes).

**Process:**
1. Define what "habituated" looks like (once per day? twice per week?).
2. Capture time-stamped behavioral data.
3. If at least 5% perform the behavior, iterate on the hook model. If 20%+ are habituated, you are in a strong position.
4. Run cohort analysis: compare cohorts before and after feature changes.
5. Look for the "smile graph" -- initial engagement drop followed by recovery as investments compound.

**North star metric for engagement: % of habituated users.**

### Ethics of Engagement Design

- Persuasion (user wants to do it) = ethical. Coercion (user does not want to do it) = unethical.
- **Protect vulnerable populations** (children, pathologically addicted).
- **Enact a use-and-abuse policy** (if used more than X hours/week, intervene).
- **Apply the regret test:** "Would the user use this product without regret, knowing everything the designer knows?" If too many would not, it fails the test.
- Coercion is not an effective long-term strategy.

## 9. Product Positioning (April Dunford Methodology)

### Definition

Positioning defines how your product is the best in the world at providing some value that a well-defined set of customers cares a lot about.

**Three litmus tests for good positioning:**
1. Easy to understand what the product is
2. Clear what value it delivers
3. Clear who it is for

**Positioning is the foundation** on which all marketing and sales is built. Every tactic (video marketing, growth hacking, campaigns) depends on positioning. Bad positioning = pushing rock uphill.

### The Five Components (in order)

#### 1. Competitive Alternatives
"If we didn't exist, what would a customer do?"

Broader than direct competitors. Includes status quo (Excel, manual processes, interns, doing nothing). In B2B, 40% of purchase processes end in "no decision."

**Process:**
1. Separate good-fit from bad-fit customers (use NPS for first pass).
2. Ask good-fit customers: "What were you using before? Who else was on your shortlist?"
3. Categorize alternatives by how they solve the problem (e.g., manual/Sharepoint, CMS approach, LMS approach).

#### 2. Unique Attributes
"What do we have that the competition doesn't?"

List every differentiating feature, capability, pricing model, or industry expertise. Compare against each group of competitive alternatives. If they share it, discard. Do not filter at this stage -- get everything on the board.

Source: Product teams know best what is differentiated.

#### 3. Differentiated Value
"What do those features do for the customer?"

The holy grail of positioning. Connects unique features to customer goals.

**Four-step discovery process:**
1. Pick a big-deal differentiating feature.
2. Translate to value using "So what?" (keep asking until you reach obvious customer value while maintaining differentiation).
3. Map features to value themes (multiple features may support the same theme).
4. Align on 1-3 overarching value themes.

**Pitfalls:**
- Going too far with "so whats" and losing differentiation (sounding like everyone else).
- Confusing objection handling with value (security compliance, implementation speed are not reasons customers pick you).
- Getting hung up on wording instead of conceptual agreement.

#### 4. Best-Fit Customers
"Who cares most about this value?"

Segmentation based on observable characteristics that make customers care about your differentiated value. Not demographics or firmographics alone.

**Process:**
1. Identify characteristics of customers most excited by your value (growth stage, geography, conferences attended, software used, business model).
2. Size the segment: Work backward from sales goal through the funnel.
3. Segment as narrowly as possible for higher marketing/sales ROI.
4. Base on current product, not wishful thinking.

**Pitfalls:** Segmenting on unmeasurable characteristics ("people who love design"). Creating personas of non-decision-makers instead of the champion.

#### 5. Market Category
"What are you? What assumptions do we trigger?"

A market category sets off a chain of assumptions about your product.

**Three positioning styles:**

| Style | When to Use | Risk Level |
|-------|------------|------------|
| **Big Fish, Small Pond** | Win an underserved subsegment of an existing category | Low -- recommended default |
| **Head to Head** | Category exists but no clear leader yet | High -- requires deep pockets |
| **New Game** | Create a new category | Very high -- category creators frequently do not survive to win |

**Big Fish, Small Pond** examples: Salesforce (small business CRM), Baidu (search in China), Freshworks (multi-brand customer support).

**New Game** warning: Involves extensive customer education. After years of evangelizing, fresh-funded competitors enter. "That's why we're not using Ask Jeeves. That's why nobody knows what a Creative MP3 player is."

### The Sales Story (Testing Positioning)

A sales narrative with two phases:

**Phase 1: Setup (educate on the market)**
1. Frame the problem (inverse of your value)
2. Evaluate alternative approaches (highlight the gap)
3. Visualize a perfect world (define purchase criteria matching your value)

**Phase 2: Follow-Through (demonstrate your product)**
4. Who we are (brief company/category introduction)
5. Value we deliver (mapped from positioning exercise, often via demo)
6. Proof (metrics, case studies, third-party validation)
7. The ask (clear next step -- never leave without one)

**Testing process:**
1. Train one sales rep on the new pitch.
2. Run 7-12 real pitches with qualified prospects (not existing customers).
3. Pass/fail: The rep says "This is better than the old pitch."
4. Use that rep to train others (reps trust peers, not marketing).
5. Only then hand positioning to marketing for messaging and campaigns.

## 10. Measuring Product Success

### Framework Integration

Measuring Product Success uses the same DHM/GEM/SMT frameworks applied systematically:

1. **Define high-level engagement metric** (retention for subscription, MAU for freemium, transactions for e-commerce).
2. **Prioritize with GEM** -- Align executive team on growth, engagement, monetization.
3. **Connect with SMT** -- Each strategy gets a proxy metric and tactics.
4. **Each team/pod has its own SMT** with proxy metrics rolling up to the company-level metric.

### Proxy Metric Template

"The percentage of [new or returning] customers who engage in a [minimum threshold of value] within [a certain time period]."

Examples:
- Netflix: % of members rating 50+ movies in first 2 months
- The metric should be measurable, non-gameable, discrete, represent value creation, useful in A/B tests, and correlated with the high-level metric.

### Consumer Science

Four sources of data that progressively build confidence:
1. Qualitative (focus groups, interviews)
2. Quantitative (surveys, NPS, existing data analysis)
3. A/B testing (fast-paced experimentation)
4. Causation testing (controlled experiments proving the proxy metric drives the engagement metric)

### Key Principle

A culture that embraces risk-taking and experimentation is essential. Product leaders define strategy, then give swim lane owners time (3-6 months) to find the right proxy metric. It is costly to have a team focused on the wrong metric.
