# Product Strategy Frameworks Reference

Source: Section School Product Strategy Sprint (Gibson Biddle), Measuring Product Success, Building Product Roadmaps

## 1. The DHM Framework

### Definition

The DHM framework (Delight, Hard-to-copy, Margin-enhancing) is a step-by-step approach to building product strategy. It answers three core questions that form the foundation of any product strategy:

1. **D -- How will your product delight customers?**
2. **H -- What will make it hard for competitors to copy?**
3. **M -- How will it generate higher profits or margins?**

**Strategy defined:** "A route to continuing power in a significant market." (Hamilton Helmer)

Product strategy enables leaders to communicate an inspired vision of the future, form testable hypotheses, blend discipline and chaos, and facilitate prioritization.

### D -- Delight Customers

Delight is the reason customers return -- your product solves a problem or satisfies a need so well they cannot live without it.

**Three ways to create delight:**

1. **Remove inefficiencies** -- Save time, reduce cost, improve effectiveness. Netflix eliminated the Blockbuster-era pain of driving to a store, limited selection, and late fees.
2. **Satisfy an important need extremely well** -- Picture yourself at 8 PM on a weeknight wanting to fade into the couch. Netflix makes that effortless.
3. **Deliver unexpected benefits** -- Benefits beyond what customers would think to request. Before Netflix made binge-watching and autoplay mainstream, nobody thought of it as part of TV-watching.

### H -- Hard-to-Copy Advantage

Strategies that competitors cannot easily replicate. Four major "powers":

| Power | Description | Netflix Example |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Brand** | Trust through promise-keeping increases loyalty | "Movie enjoyment made easy" -- 200M+ members trust Netflix with credit cards |
| **Network Effects** | More usage improves the product | Viewing data improves recommendations; device ecosystem (Xbox partnership, pre-wired on every screen) |
| **Economies of Scale** | Large user base spreads costs, enabling higher investment | Content costs amortized across 200M+ members; can invest more than Amazon or Apple |
| **Unique Technology** | Proprietary capabilities competitors lack | Personalization engine -- 80%+ of what members watch is facilitated by recommendation algorithms |

### M -- Margin-Enhancing (Superior Margins)

Margin = Price minus Cost to produce. Improved by raising perceived value (enabling higher prices) or reducing/optimizing costs.

**Netflix business model evolution:**
- DVD transactional (a la carte) --> subscription (1999)
- Tested advertising on website and DVD envelopes (2005)
- Streaming with hourly cap --> unlimited streaming (2007)
- Ad-supported tier (2022)

**Cost-side advantage:** Using audience data to "right-size" original content investment. Netflix forecasted 100M members would watch Stranger Things (invested $500M). Predicted 20M for BoJack Horseman (invested $100M).

### How to Form Product Strategy Hypotheses (3-Step Process)

1. **Brainstorm** -- Use the three DHM questions as prompts. Generate raw ideas for how to delight, build defensibility, and enhance margins.
2. **Affinity map** -- Group similar ideas together to form high-level hypotheses. Name each hypothesis (e.g., "Personalization," "Original Content," "Device Ecosystem").
3. **Prioritize in a matrix** -- Compare hypotheses against the three DHM components. Prioritize strategies that achieve two or more components.

**Key mindset:** Product strategies are intentionally framed as hypotheses. At Netflix, half of all high-level product strategy hypotheses failed over five years. That was still sufficient to build a world-class product.

### The DHM Flywheel

The three factors create a reinforcing cycle:
- More delight --> more perceived value --> higher prices possible
- Higher margin --> more profits --> more investment in innovation
- More innovation --> better product --> more delight
- The cycle continues indefinitely

**Key lessons:**
- Experiment with different business models over the product's life; you are never done
- Be customer-obsessed, not competitor-obsessed
- Netflix evolved from DVD-by-mail to worldwide streaming to major TV/movie studio

## 2. The GEM Framework

### Definition

GEM (Growth, Engagement, Monetization) is a cross-functional prioritization model that forces alignment across product, marketing, and sales on which of the three factors to prioritize at a given time. Misalignment on GEM is one of the biggest causes of organizational dysfunction.

### The Three Factors

| Factor | Definition | Example Metrics |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Growth** | Expanding the market and attracting more customers | Year-over-year user growth |
| **Engagement** | Proxy for customer delight and product quality ("Is my product better?") | Varies by business model (see below) |
| **Monetization** | Finding a profitable model where revenues exceed costs | Customer lifetime value (LTV), gross margin, monthly revenue per customer |

### Engagement Metrics by Business Model

- **Ad-based** (Facebook/Instagram): Monthly Active Users (MAU), shifting to Daily Active Users (DAU) as product matures
- **Subscription** (Netflix/Spotify): Retention (monthly or annual)
- **E-commerce** (Amazon): Paid transactions, tied to minimum spend over time
- **Freemium** (Spotify): Monthly Active Users (MAU)

### How GEM Priorities Shift Over a Company's Lifecycle

- **Early-stage startups** alternate between engagement and growth, typically delaying monetization until engagement is strong. Instagram waited five years before experimenting with ads.
- **Growth stage** shifts to growth or monetization depending on investor and market pressure.
- Priorities should be revisited every six months.

### Netflix GEM Case Study

| Year | Priority | Why | Metric |
|------|----------|-----|--------|
| 2005 | Monetization | ~2M users growing 30% YoY, challenge was profitability | Customer Lifetime Value |
| 2008 | Growth | Confident in profitability; goal of 20M subs by 2010 | Subscriber count |
| 2022 | Growth | First subscriber loss in 10 years; content flywheel needs scale | YoY member growth |

### GEM Process

1. Have individual conversations with the CEO and functional leaders about how they would prioritize the three factors.
2. Compare perspectives across functions.
3. Debate differences and reach agreement.
4. Prioritize once at the company level -- not differently by each function.
5. Repeat every six months.

### Key Principles

- The high-level engagement metric defines "is our product better?" but should not always be called a "North Star" because companies may sometimes prioritize growth or monetization above engagement.
- Strong GEM prioritization creates a reinforcing system: engagement drives growth (better product = word of mouth), growth supports profit, profit funds product improvement.
- Shared GEM language reduces politics and focuses teams on agreed outcomes.
- The most important requirement is that the team agrees. There is no perfect answer -- pick a priority and act.

## 3. The SMT Framework

### Definition

SMT (Strategy, Metric, Tactic) connects product strategy with measurable proxy metrics and concrete experiments. It is the operational bridge between high-level strategy and day-to-day execution.

### The Problem It Solves

High-level engagement metrics (e.g., retention) are:
- **Hard to move** -- Netflix retention improved from 5% monthly cancel rate (2005) to 2% (2022). That is one percentage point every five years.
- **Lagging indicators** -- It takes months before you have solid data on whether customers stay.

Therefore, teams need **proxy metrics** -- leading indicators that signal whether a strategy is working.

### The Three Components

```
Strategy --> Proxy Metric --> Tactics
(What)       (How to measure)  (What to build/test)
```

1. **Strategy** -- A high-level product hypothesis drawn from DHM brainstorming. Example: "Personalization will improve retention by helping members find something to watch faster."
2. **Proxy Metric** -- A testable, precise leading indicator that correlates with the high-level engagement metric. Example: "% of new members who rate at least 50 movies in their first two months."
3. **Tactics** -- Concrete projects or experiments designed to move the proxy metric. Example: "Build a ratings wizard. Develop recommendation algorithms."

### Netflix SMT Example (Personalization)

| Component | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **High-level engagement metric** | Monthly retention |
| **Strategy** | Personalization |
| **Problem observed** | Members browsed hundreds of titles before choosing |
| **Hypothesis** | More ratings --> better recommendations --> less time wasted --> higher retention |
| **Proxy metric** | % of new members who rate at least 50 movies in first 2 months |
| **Why it works** | Precise, testable, already showed correlation with retention |
| **Tactics** | Ratings wizard, recommendation algorithms, taste profiles |

### Swim Lane SMTs

The same SMT framework applies at every level of the organization:

- **Company-level SMT** -- Aligns all teams to a common engagement metric
- **Swim lane / pod SMT** -- Each product leader defines their own strategy, proxy metrics, and tactics

At Netflix, the personalization swim lane had sub-strategies each with their own proxy metrics:
- Taste data collection (explicit ratings, queue behavior, completion rates)
- Content attribute data (genre, cast, tone, detailed descriptors)
- Matching algorithms connecting member tastes to movie attributes
- RMSE (root mean squared error) as a proxy for recommendation accuracy

### Proxy Metric Criteria

A strong proxy metric should be:

1. **Measurable** -- You can capture the data
2. **Non-gameable** -- Cannot be artificially inflated
3. **Discrete** -- Specific enough to be actionable
4. **Represents customer value creation** -- Moving it creates genuine value
5. **Useful in A/B tests** -- Can serve as the success metric for experiments
6. **Correlated with high-level engagement metric** -- Eventually want to prove causation

### Finding the Right Proxy Metric

1. Start from what makes your product more engaging (return to customer delight).
2. Form a hypothesis about what behavior signals value.
3. Capture time-stamped data to measure the behavior.
4. Verify correlation with the high-level engagement metric.
5. Eventually prove causation via A/B testing.
6. Allow 3-6 months to identify, move, and validate a proxy metric.

### The Consumer Science Engine

Effective product strategy is "supercharged" by consumer science:

1. **Qualitative data** -- Rich picture of customer needs, likes, dislikes (focus groups, interviews)
2. **Quantitative data** -- Triangulate with behavioral data to understand past and current patterns (surveys, NPS, existing data)
3. **Fast-paced experimentation** -- A/B testing to fuel learning
4. **Correlation then causation** -- Show proxy metrics correlate with engagement metric, then prove causation via controlled experiments

Netflix ran a major A/B test in 2012 comparing a non-personalized site to the personalized experience, proving personalization improved retention. This was described as a "10-year leap of faith" where steady proxy metric improvement provided confidence along the way.

## 4. Product Roadmaps

### Purpose

Roadmaps matter for two reasons:
1. **Planning tool** -- Gives stakeholders a sense of time and how work fits together
2. **Communication tool** -- Expresses product strategy and rallies everyone around a shared vision

A roadmap is an expression of your product strategy. Build it last, after defining strategy.

### Two Types of Roadmaps

#### Project-Based Roadmaps (What and How)

- A prioritized list of projects across the next four quarters.
- Structure: High-level strategies on the left, projects sequenced quarter by quarter.
- Best for communicating product vision to executives and investors.
- Tension: Creates expectations about delivery dates, but experimentation generates learning that changes plans.

**Handling uncertainty:** Make a "no-commitment commitment" -- be clear that near-term items are more certain and later items will likely change. "I'm confident about each of the projects in the very near future, but my assumption is that we will learn so much in the coming months that future plans will change."

#### Outcome-Based Roadmaps (Flexible Alternative)

- Instead of committing to projects and dates, commit to outcomes via proxy metrics.
- Structure: High-level strategies --> proxy metric outcome --> projects on a "now / next / consider" timeline.
- Keeps focus on moving key metrics without overcommitting.
- Shifts conversation from "build this feature" to "what problem are we solving and what outcome matters?"

### Prioritization: Impact vs. Effort

- **Impact** -- Value to customers and company relative to GEM priorities. Non-negotiable.
- **Effort** -- Cost, time, and resources. Area of tradeoffs. Reduce scope to capture maximum value with least resources.
- Take a SWAG (Stupid Wild-Ass Guess) on effort, then validate with design and engineering.

### Netflix Ratings Wizard Example

The ratings wizard was high-impact, low-effort: familiar star-rating UI, quickly prompted users to rate movies for better personalization. The team did not over-invest in design -- they just wanted to get it up and running fast.

### Practical Roadmap Principles

1. **Do not promise delivery** for projects your team is not confident about.
2. **Use the roadmap to say no** -- "Where does this fit? What metric does it move? How does it align with priorities?"
3. **Build roadmaps last** -- After DHM brainstorming, GEM prioritization, and SMT lockup. The roadmap expresses strategy; it is not a list of disconnected projects.
4. **At any point, you should be able to explain why** the company is focused on a specific project, connecting it back to strategy.

## 5. Putting It All Together: The Complete Framework Sequence

```
Step 1: DHM Brainstorm
  "How will we delight? What's hard to copy? How do we enhance margins?"
  --> Generate product strategy hypotheses

Step 2: GEM Prioritization
  "Growth, Engagement, or Monetization first?"
  --> Align cross-functional teams on company priorities
  --> Define the high-level engagement metric

Step 3: SMT Lockup
  "Strategy --> Proxy Metric --> Tactics"
  --> Connect each strategy to testable leading indicators
  --> Brainstorm projects/experiments for each

Step 4: Roadmap
  "What are we doing, when, and why?"
  --> Project-based or outcome-based
  --> Prioritized by impact vs. effort
  --> Express strategy, not isolated features
```

### Netflix Complete Example

| Framework | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| **DHM** | Delight: convenience, selection, personalization. Hard-to-copy: brand, network effects, scale, unique tech. Margin: subscription model, right-sizing content investment |
| **GEM** | 2005: Monetization first. 2008: Growth first. 2022: Growth first |
| **SMT** | Strategy: Personalization. Metric: % rating 50+ movies in 2 months. Tactics: ratings wizard, algorithms |
| **Roadmap** | Personalization (ratings wizard in Q1), content expansion, device ecosystem |

### Key Principles Across All Frameworks

1. **Product strategies are hypotheses** -- Many will fail. At Netflix, half failed. That is acceptable.
2. **Metrics connect at every level** -- Company priorities --> product strategy --> swim lane strategies. Everyone measures success consistently.
3. **Consumer science drives decisions** -- Qualitative + quantitative + experimentation. Intuition is good; evidence is better.
4. **Iteration never stops** -- Business models evolve, priorities shift, strategies are refined. You are never done.
5. **Be customer-obsessed, not competitor-obsessed** -- Play your own game.
6. **Alignment is the most important outcome** -- Shared language around metrics makes collaboration easier and decisions less political.
