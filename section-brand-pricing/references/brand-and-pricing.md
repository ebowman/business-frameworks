## Part 1: Brand Strategy

### What Is a Brand?

Brand exists in the hearts and minds of customers -- the intangible associations wrapped around a given set of products or services. You cannot drop a brand on your toe. These associations give consumers the ability to express themselves and serve as a shortcut for decision-making.

**Brand is Latin for margin.** Ideally, irrational margin. Show me a company with great brands, I will show you a company with above-market margins. Branding is getting people to make irrational decisions.

The strongest brands in the world use brand as a guiding light for everyone to understand communication, behavior, and capital allocation -- creating a whole that is greater than the sum of its parts.

---

### The Three Lines of Business Framework

A framework explaining how brand unlocks stakeholder value through three lines:

1. **Cost to Produce** (bottom line) -- what it takes to make the product
2. **Price** (middle line) -- what customers pay
3. **Perceived Value** (top line) -- what customers believe it is worth

**Two ways to create value:**
- **Push costs down:** Only 1-2 firms per industry can win this way (Southwest, Dell, Walmart, Costco)
- **Increase perceived value:** Advertising, artisanship, R&D push the top line up. This is what brand strategy is about.

When strong branding increases perceived value, you can raise prices OR keep them the same while widening margin. Brand creates emotional associations that lead to irrational decision-making in your favor.

---

### The Clock Model

A framework to evaluate every brand interaction across the customer journey, visualized as a clock face.

**12-4: Pre-Purchase**
- Advertising, tradeshows, events, sponsorships, social media, PR
- Goal: create desire, awareness, intent
- Best-in-class: Nike (advertising), Tesla/Elon Musk (earned media/PR)

**4-8: Purchase**
- Distribution, packaging, store design, user reviews, UGC, financing
- Goal: the first physical or digital contact point
- Best-in-class: The Gap (store experience), Starbucks (democratized Italian coffee shop)

**8-12: Post-Purchase**
- Customer service, loyalty programs, warranties, community
- Goal: reinforce the decision, build loyalty, drive retention
- Best-in-class: FedEx (package tracking), Hyundai (7-year unconditional warranty)

**Key principles:**
- Brand-building occurs anywhere a customer interacts with your brand
- Most firms cannot invest in all three zones -- typically invest to be best-of-breed in one and outsource the other two
- Historical trend: capital is migrating from pre-purchase (advertising) toward purchase (distribution) and post-purchase (technology, data-driven service)
- The "gangster move" (boring but effective) is investing in post-purchase
- Put the customer in the center and ask: with limited capital, where can I have the greatest ROI relative to competitors in my sector?

**B2B application:** Same dynamics apply. Advertising is less effective. B2B invests heavily in sales teams (purchase) and post-purchase with high-ticket items (customer service, relationship building, operational reviews).

### The Three Hurdles Framework

Every strategic decision, capital allocation, and brand effort must clear three hurdles:

**1. Differentiation**
- Do we stand out vs. other similar offerings?
- Are we truly different?
- Challenge: primary means of differentiation (marketing/branding) is getting more expensive and less effective as consumers opt out of advertising and rely on reviews

**2. Relevance**
- Does anyone care?
- Are our areas of differentiation relevant to the market?
- Challenge: differentiation and relevance are often in tension. Ferrari is highly differentiated but relevant to very few. CPG products are relevant but struggle to differentiate.

**3. Sustainability**
- How do we own it?
- How do we ensure that after competition discovers our advantage, we can maintain it?

**Moats for sustainability:**
- Legal barriers: patents, copyrights, trademarks, operating licenses
- One-of-a-kind strategic assets: locations, human talent, trade secrets, IP, brand names
- Economies of scale, market scale, sunk costs
- Information gaps and complexity
- Increasing returns advantages

**Modern sustainability increasingly comes from:**
- Early leadership in a category (Google in search)
- Human capital investment ("blue flame thinking")
- Access to cheap capital (Amazon's ability to out-invest competitors)
- Culture of quality (HBO attracting the best talent)

**Case study in unsustainability:** The bed-in-a-box market -- differentiating and relevant, but not sustainable. 175 companies selling mattresses from the same factory, stealing each other's direct-to-consumer acquisition strategies.

**Personal application:** Evaluate yourself on the three hurdles. What is different about you? Why does the organization care? What is it about you that no one else can replicate?

### The Brand Identity Model

Based on David Aaker's Brand Vision Model. A framework to articulate what a brand is, making the intangible tangible.

**Three concentric layers:**

**A. Brand Essence (Core)**
- The one thing you want people to think of immediately -- a reflexive reaction
- Must be: aspirational, simple, visceral
- Examples: Oprah = friend. Disney = family. Southwest = freedom. Jet Blue = humanity.

**B. Core Identity (Middle Layer)**
- Must be: timeless, important, substantive
- The 3-4 associations that just miss being the essence
- Provides texture and substance
- Allows competing companies with similar essences to carve out distinct positions
- **How to determine:** List associations, apply the Three Hurdles. Whichever clears the hurdles by the greatest margin becomes the essence; the rest form core identity.

**C. Extended Identity (Outer Layer)**
- Elaborations that bring the brand to life through four lenses:
  - **Brand as Person:** Personify the brand to understand its character. ("If Disney and Nike had a baby, what would that person look/smell/behave like?")
  - **Brand as Symbol:** Logo, sound, color. We hold onto symbols more easily than words. (Intel's sound, McDonald's arches, Disney's ears)
  - **Brand as Product:** The one product that manifests the brand's essence. (GE = lightbulb. Tesla = Model S.)
  - **Brand as Organization:** Associations with culture. (GE = training ground. Levi's = community-minded.)

**Creating your brand identity -- 3-step process:**
1. **Conduct a Brand Audit:** Identify current positive and negative associations. Ask: What is the current value proposition? Point of differentiation? How do customers perceive the brand? Read reviews, talk to customers.
2. **Fix the Negatives:** Address negative associations before constructing identity. (Facebook cannot highlight "connection" until it addresses privacy/security.)
3. **Elevate the Positives:** Form the basis of brand identity. Should be aspirational -- just a little out of reach.

### Quality Cues

A product characteristic that serves as a shortcut to signal value to consumers. Best-in-class brands invest in 1-3 quality cues that they become known for.

**Types of quality cues:**
- **Pricing:** Coming in at a higher price signals value (Grey Goose Vodka)
- **Product traits:** Hotel lobby feel, automobile door closing sound, towel thickness
- **Packaging and warranties:** Tiffany's aquamarine box, Hyundai's 7-year warranty
- **Location/origin:** Cheese from Wisconsin, wine from Champagne, cars from Germany

**How to identify the right quality cues:**
1. Start with the Clock Model -- where could you over-invest to signal quality?
2. Find something with outsized impact relative to investment
3. Gut-check against the Three Hurdles (differentiated, relevant, sustainable)

### Laddering

The act of focusing on a brand trait of yours that highlights a weakness of the competition, making your brand look even better.

**Why it works:** "If you walk into a room and say 'hey look how tall I am,' you are also saying how short everyone else is." It kills two birds with one stone.

**Best-in-class example:** Apple and privacy. "Privacy is a basic human right." This is a strength for Apple AND a weakness for Google and Facebook. Apple can own this because they walk the walk. It clears all three hurdles: differentiated, relevant (consumers care more each year), and sustainable (Apple's business model does not depend on data harvesting).

**Case study -- Aerie vs. Victoria's Secret:**
Aerie registered double-digit revenue growth every quarter for six years. Market share grew from less than 2% to 7%, while Victoria's Secret dropped from 79% to 65%.

Three laddering strategies:
1. **Product:** 97%+ of Aerie products available in XXS to XXL (vs. only 4% of VS in XXS, 10% in XXL). Widened addressable market while making VS look exclusive/elitist.
2. **Marketing:** Stopped photoshopping models in 2014. Featured "real women" of all body types. Capitalized on 300%+ growth in "body positivity" searches. Made VS's airbrushed model ads look antiquated.
3. **Events:** AerieReal Summit at $25/ticket with proceeds to National Eating Disorders Association (vs. VS fashion show at $18K+/ticket with celebrity front rows). VS canceled its show in 2019.

**How to apply laddering:**
1. List ways you could ladder the competition
2. Apply the Three Hurdles to each option
3. Focus on the one that clears all three hurdles by the widest margin
4. Resist the temptation to use multiple messages -- even the biggest brands need one core strategic message

### Brand Architecture

The organizing structure of your brand portfolio.

**Four organizing principles:**

1. **Master Brand (Branded House):** One brand across all products. FedEx, Apple, NYU, McKinsey. Every product includes the master brand.

2. **Sub-Brands:** New category with huge opportunity, but you still want parent brand equity. Toyota Camry vs. Tundra vs. Prius.

3. **Endorsed Brands:** "By" or "from" a parent brand. Polo by Ralph Lauren. Fairfield by Marriott. Used when a new brand does not fit but you want to leverage master brand equity.

4. **Invisible Brands:** Used in large conglomerates when brands do not fit together. Hidden Valley Ranch (owned by Clorox). Coach (was owned by Sara Lee).

**Key principles:**
- **Have a bias toward fewer brands and a master-brand portfolio.** The trend has shifted from 60% "House of Brands" in 1990 to 70% "Branded House" in 2020. Managing multiple brands is expensive and diverts capital.
- **Launching new brands is a rookie marketing move.** Each brand is expensive and may confuse customers.
- **The cardinal rule: clarity for consumers.** The best architectures make sense to the customer (e.g., American Express, American Express Platinum, American Express Black).

**Five reasons to launch a new sub-brand:**
1. Access a new, distinct customer segment (Old Navy for young mothers)
2. Enter new markets horizontally (Office, Xbox, Microsoft Dynamics)
3. Specific value proposition (Diet Coke)
4. New associations (Mazda Miata)
5. Eliminate channel conflict (WPP's different studio brands)

### Crisis Management

Three rules of crisis management:

1. **Top person responds** -- the CEO must be seen handling the issue
2. **Acknowledge the issue** -- do not minimize, deflect, or threaten
3. **Overcorrect** -- take visible, decisive action beyond what seems necessary

**Case studies:**

| Company | Response | Outcome |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Tylenol/J&J (1982) | CEO acted immediately, cleared every shelf, spent $100M recalling | Stock hit 52-week high weeks later |
| Exxon Valdez | CEO absent. VP handled. Delayed acknowledgment. | Textbook "how not to" for decades |
| Facebook/Cambridge Analytica | Mark & Sheryl absent for a week. Threatened the Guardian. | Stock crashed. Became new "how not to" |
| Walmart (Texas shooting) | CEO responded immediately, stopped selling ammunition, limited open carry | Stock fell 3%, rebounded next day |

**Personal application:** When someone is upset -- address it, acknowledge it, and overcorrect. Our initial reaction is to dig in and be combative, but that only escalates.

## Part 2: Behavioral Pricing (Adam Alter)

### Two Lenses for Pricing

**1. Economics of Pricing**
- What is the range of acceptable prices?
- How can pricing decisions drive customer lifetime value?

**2. Psychology of Pricing**
- Economic concepts alone ignore that consumers are human
- Behavioral factors significantly influence price perception and willingness to pay

### Key Pricing Concepts

**Willingness to Pay (WTP):**
- The maximum amount a consumer will pay for a product
- Can be measured through auction methods, experimental methods, and surveys
- WTP is not fixed -- it is influenced by framing, context, and psychological factors

**Pricing as a Quality Cue:**
- Higher price can signal higher quality (Grey Goose effect)
- Under-pricing can actually reduce demand by signaling low quality

**Customer Lifetime Value (CLV):**
- Pricing decisions should consider not just the initial transaction but the total value of the customer relationship
- Sometimes charging less initially creates more long-term value

### Recommended Resources
- "The Strategy and Tactics of Pricing" by Nagle, Hogan, and Zale
- "Pricing Strategy" by T. Smith
- "Power Pricing" by Dolan and Simon
- "Smart Pricing" by Raju and Zhang
- "PRICELESS: The Myth of Fair Value" by Poundstone

## Part 3: Customer Retention Strategies

### Churn Fundamentals

**Baseline churn rates:** 3-8% monthly for stable businesses, higher for startups.

**Customer engagement measurement:** Recency / Frequency / Depth / Breadth

### Types of Churn

**By voluntariness:**
- **Voluntary (Active) churn:** Customer actively decides to leave
- **Involuntary (Passive) churn:** Customer leaves for reasons like credit card expiration (can be ~2% alone)

**By acceptability:**
- **Acceptable churn:** Natural, expected reasons you would not try to resolve (outgrew diapers, moved away from gym, company changed business model)
- **Unacceptable churn:** Customer left for reasons you could and should have prevented

### Common Causes of Unacceptable Churn
- B2B2C: Buyer purchases but users never adopt ("employees never figure out how to use it, continue using Excel")
- Streaming: customer cannot find exciting content
- Poor experience: customer has a bad interaction with staff
- Boredom: product fails to maintain engagement

### The 3-Step Churn Reduction Process

1. **Determine your types and rates of churn.** Break down churn by voluntary/involuntary and acceptable/unacceptable.
2. **Prioritize drivers of churn by impact and cost of mitigation.** Not all churn is equal -- focus on high-impact, cost-effective fixes first.
3. **Fix the leaks in the bucket, leak by leak.** Systematic, incremental approach rather than trying to solve everything at once.

## Quick-Reference: Brand Strategy Checklist

- [ ] Identify where your brand creates value using the Three Lines framework
- [ ] Map the Clock Model: where do you invest (pre-purchase, purchase, post-purchase)?
- [ ] Evaluate every strategic decision against the Three Hurdles (differentiated, relevant, sustainable)
- [ ] Conduct a brand audit (positives and negatives)
- [ ] Define brand identity: essence, core identity, extended identity
- [ ] Identify 1-3 quality cues to invest in
- [ ] Evaluate laddering opportunities against your primary competitor
- [ ] Review brand architecture for clarity and efficiency
- [ ] Prepare a crisis management playbook (top person, acknowledge, overcorrect)
- [ ] Analyze pricing through both economic and behavioral lenses
- [ ] Measure and categorize churn; fix leaks systematically

## Key Takeaways

1. **Brand is about perceived value, not product features.** The goal is to create emotional associations that lead to irrational margin.

2. **Capital allocation follows the clock.** The modern trend is migrating investment from pre-purchase advertising toward purchase distribution and post-purchase technology.

3. **Every strategy must clear three hurdles:** differentiated, relevant, and sustainable. If it fails any one, rethink.

4. **Laddering is the most efficient competitive weapon.** One message that highlights your strength AND exposes the competitor's weakness.

5. **Have a bias toward fewer brands.** The Branded House model wins in the modern economy.

6. **Crisis response has three immutable rules.** Top person responds, acknowledge the issue, overcorrect. Companies that follow these rules recover; those that do not may never recover.

7. **Fix churn leak by leak.** Distinguish voluntary from involuntary, acceptable from unacceptable, and prioritize by impact and cost.
